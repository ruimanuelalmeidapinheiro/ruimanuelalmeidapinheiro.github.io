“Is IQ related to the cognitive system?”

“Yes, IQ (Intelligence Quotient) is deeply related to the cognitive system, but it’s important to understand that it is not the system itself; it is a measure of performance of certain functions within that system.

Think of the cognitive system as the ‘hardware and operating system’ of your brain, and IQ as a benchmark test that measures the speed and efficiency of some specific tasks.

Here’s how this relationship is structured:

1. Cognitive Functions Measured by IQ IQ tests focus on specific domains of the cognitive system:

  • Working Memory: The capacity to retain and manipulate information in the short term (such as solving a mental calculation).**

  • Processing Speed: The time the system takes to identify, react, and make decisions about stimuli.**

  • Fluid Reasoning: The ability to solve new problems, identify patterns, and use logic without relying on prior knowledge.**

  • Crystallized Intelligence: Access to accumulated information and vocabulary stock.**

2. The ‘g’ Factor (General Intelligence) In cognitive psychology, there is the concept of factor g. The theory suggests that all cognitive tasks share a common base. If your cognitive system is very efficient in visual logic, there is a high probability that it will also be efficient in verbal comprehension, because the underlying biological processes (neurons, synapses, myelination) are the same.

3. The Limitations of the System Although IQ measures the ‘power’ of the cognitive system, it ignores other fundamental parts of mental architecture:

  • Social Cognition: The capacity to interpret others’ intentions and emotions.**

  • Creativity: The generation of solutions outside of testable logical patterns.**

  • Rationality: Curiously, having a high IQ does not guarantee that a person thinks rationally. Someone can have a fast ‘processor’ but use it to justify unfounded beliefs (what we call dysrationalia).”**
