📜 Understanding the Babylonian Base-60 System

The Babylonians inherited and refined a sexagesimal system from earlier Mesopotamian civilizations like the Sumerians. It had several key characteristics:

  • Base 60: It used 60 as its primary base, making it a highly composite and divisible number.

  • Positional System: It was one of the earliest known positional numeral systems, where a digit’s value depends on its position.

  • Two Basic Symbols: They only needed two cuneiform symbols—a vertical wedge (𒁹) for ‘1’ and a sideways wedge (𒌋) for ‘10’—to construct numbers from 1 to 59.

✋ The Theory of Finger-Counting to 60

Your question touches on a known historical theory about how such a large base might have been used for counting. One plausible method involves counting on the fingers and knuckles of both hands:

  1. Using One Hand for Base-12: On one hand (excluding the thumb), you can use your thumb to count the three joints on each of the four fingers. This allows you to count to 12 (4 fingers × 3 joints).

  2. Using the Other Hand as a Tally: The five fingers (including the thumb) on the other hand can then be used to track how many full sets of 12 you have counted (1, 12, 24, 36, 48).

  3. Reaching 60: By combining these, you could count up to 5 × 12 = 60. This provides a practical, physical explanation for why a base-60 system might have developed from a base-12 subsystem.

Note: While this finger-counting theory is often discussed, it is important to know that it is a modern hypothesis. I could not find primary historical or archaeological evidence directly proving the Babylonians used this exact method. The main evidence for the system comes from thousands of surviving clay tablets.

0️⃣ The Challenge of “Zero”

A significant feature of the early Babylonian system was the absence of a true zero symbol. This created ambiguity:

  • The same symbol for ‘1’ could mean 1, 60, or 3600, with meaning inferred from context.

  • Later (around 700-300 BC), they developed a placeholder symbol (often two small wedges: ) to indicate an empty position, but only for gaps in the middle of a number, not at the end. Therefore, numbers like 60 (1,0) and 1 (1) could still look identical.

🔢 Writing and Calculating with Base-60

To illustrate, here is how numbers were formed and calculated using this system:

  • Forming Numbers 1-59: A number like 23 was written as a group of symbols: 𒌋𒌋 (two “tens” = 20) followed by 𒁹𒁹𒁹 (three “ones” = 3).

  • Positional Notation for Larger Numbers: In a positional system, the same digit represents different powers of 60. For example, the cuneiform for “1, 2, 3” would represent 1×60² + 2×60¹ + 3×60⁰, which is 3600 + 120 + 3 = 3723 in decimal.

  • Mathematical Operations: They performed complex math. A key tool was extensive tables of reciprocals (pairs of numbers that multiply to 60 or a power of 60), which simplified division. They also used formulas that relied on tables of squares to perform multiplication.

🕒 A Lasting Legacy

The most direct legacy of the sexagesimal system is in our modern world:

  • Time: 60 seconds in a minute, 60 minutes in an hour.

  • Geometry: 360 degrees (6 × 60) in a circle, with each degree subdivided into 60 arcminutes and 60 arcseconds.
