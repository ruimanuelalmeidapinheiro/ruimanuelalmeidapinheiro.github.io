The need for consistency

This article posits consistency as the non-negotiable foundation of reliable knowledge, functional systems, and ethical action. In deterministic reasoning, inconsistency signals error—not ambiguity. In information systems, it ensures data integrity across distributed states; in law, it prevents arbitrary enforcement; in science, it enables falsifiability and cumulative progress. Drawing on Asimov's logical rigor and Orwell's demand for linguistic precision, the piece argues that consistency is not rigidity but coherence: the alignment of premises, methods, and conclusions across time and context. For the individual, it manifests as intellectual honesty—admitting error, revising beliefs, resisting cognitive dissonance. Societally, consistency in rules and expectations reduces friction, builds trust, and allows complexity to emerge without collapse. The inverse fails: no amount of goodwill or innovation compensates for foundational incoherence.
