1. Memory Storage of Agents: The Bioelectric Network

The Core Idea:
Memory is stored in bioelectric networks—not in genes, not in synapses, but in electrical patterns that connect cells together .

How It Works (Simple Version):

  • Think of your body as having two networks: the brain network (neurons) for navigating 3D space, and the somatic network (all other cells) for navigating “anatomical space” (body shape)

  • These electrical networks store pattern memories—templates of what the body should look like

  • Just as your brain stores memories of places you’ve been, your cells store memories of shapes they should build

The Key Insight:

“These pattern memories are encoded in the electrical network of the body of the early embryo and subsequent exactly in the way that we think of as memories about navigating three-dimensional space are encoded in the brain” — Michael Levin

What This Means:

  • Software, not hardware: The same cells can build different structures without rewiring—just by changing electrical patterns

  • Persistent memory: Two-headed flatworms retain their “two-headed memory” even after being cut and regenerating—proving memory is stored in the electrical network, not in specific cells

  • Collective memory: No single cell knows the whole plan. The memory lives in the network connections between cells

Analogy: It’s like a flock of birds. No bird knows the whole flock’s pattern, but together they create a coherent shape through their connections.

Each level has its own agenda, memory, and intelligence. The liver cell doesn’t “know” it’s part of a liver. The liver doesn’t “know” it’s part of a person. Yet both work together

“Cognitive Glue” — What holds these levels together?

  • You solve problems in your space (finding dinner, writing emails)

Downward Causation:
Higher levels can override lower levels. If you decide to drink alcohol (whole-body decision), your liver cells must adapt. If your liver is damaged, your whole-body behavior changes (you feel tired, avoid certain foods)

Let me analyze what Levin’s work reveals about whether bioelectric memories are stored for future generations.

The Short Answer: Not Directly—But There’s a Fascinating Nuance

Levin’s experiments show that somatic (body) bioelectric memories are NOT passed to offspring through the germline (sperm/egg). However, the capacity to form such memories is inherited, and there are indirect pathways that blur this boundary.

The Evidence: What Levin’s Experiments Actually Show

The Two-Headed Planarian Case

This is Levin’s flagship experiment, and it directly addresses your question

:

What happens:

  • A normal one-headed planarian is induced (via bioelectric manipulation) to have a two-headed bioelectric pattern

  • The animal looks normal (one head, one tail) because the bioelectric pattern is a latent memory—a “counterfactual” stored in the electrical network

  • When cut, all fragments regenerate two-headed offspring (from the parent’s body)

  • This continues “in perpetuity, as far as we can tell, forever” through somatic regeneration

Critical point: The offspring here are regenerated from the parent’s body fragments—this is somatic propagation, not sexual reproduction. When these planarians reproduce sexually (through germline/sperm/egg), the offspring revert to the default one-headed pattern

.

Levin explicitly states:

“The genetic information has not been changed... The genome actually gives you is some hardware that, when the juice is turned on, reliably takes on a default bioelectrical state“

The bioelectric memory is software that runs on hardware (the genome). The hardware is inherited; the specific software state is not.

The Deer Antler Case: Somatic Memory Without Germline Transfer

Levin discusses trophic memory in deer antlers

:

  • Deer injure their antler bone → a callus forms and heals

  • Antlers fall off and regrow from scratch every year

  • For 5 years, the new antlers “remember” the injury location and grow an ectopic branch

  • Eventually, the memory fades and normal growth resumes

Key insight: This is pure somatic memory. The antler is shed completely—the cells that “remember” are gone. Yet the memory persists in the deer’s body (likely in stem cell niches or bioelectric patterns in the pedicle). But this memory does not pass to the deer’s offspring. Each new generation starts with default antler patterns.

The Critical Distinction: Hardware vs. Software

The genome provides the ability to store and recall bioelectric memories, but not the specific memories themselves.

But Wait: Indirect Pathways to Inheritance

Levin’s framework suggests three indirect ways somatic information can influence future generations:

1. Epigenetic Priming

While bioelectric states themselves aren’t inherited, the epigenetic marks (chemical tags on DNA) that stabilize bioelectric patterns might be. Levin mentions “short range: epigenetic cell memory” as part of the morphogenetic memory system

. Some epigenetic marks can persist across a few generations.

2. Niche Construction

If somatic bioelectric changes alter the environment (e.g., a modified body creates different chemical signals, microbiome, or behavioral patterns), these environmental changes can affect offspring development. This is not genetic inheritance, but ecological inheritance.

3. Selection for Plasticity

If an organism repeatedly rewrites its bioelectric patterns in response to environmental challenges, evolution may select for enhanced plasticity—better hardware for storing somatic memories. Over generations, the capacity for memory improves, even if specific memories don’t transfer.

The Philosophical Punchline

Levin’s work reveals a profound separation between two inheritance systems:

  1. The Genome: Stores slow, hardwired, evolutionary information (hardware)

  2. The Bioelectric Network: Stores fast, flexible, learned information (software)

Your liver cell’s bioelectric memory of being part of a liver is not in your sperm or egg. Your children start with a blank slate—a default bioelectric pattern that says “build a standard human.” They must learn their own somatic memories during development.

However, the fact that cells can store such memories, and that these memories can persist for decades (or indefinitely through regeneration), suggests that evolution discovered the value of separating fast-learning (somatic) from slow-learning (germline) systems.

Simple Summary

The bioelectric network is personal memory—it dies with the individual (unless they regenerate asexually). The genome is cultural memory—it persists across generations but lacks specific life experience.

This is actually similar to how human culture works: your brain’s specific memories die with you, but you can write them down (external storage) or teach others (transmission). Cells don’t have writing, but they have something almost as powerful: the ability to rewrite their own hardware’s default settings—just not pass those rewrites to the next sexual generation.

The Baldwin Effect: How Somatic Competence Becomes Inherited

The Core Process (From Levin’s 2024 Paper)

“We interpret this slow but steady improvement in the structural fitness as a manifestation of the Baldwin effect, where (evolutionarily) acquired uni-cellular competencies—here, to assemble a target pattern—are shifted to hard-wired phenotypical traits.”

What happens:

  1. Generation 1-860: Cells learn somatically (via bioelectric plasticity) to build a specific structure

  2. Generation 860-1823: Evolution gradually “notices” this works and starts encoding it in the genome

Critical insight: The transition happens without changing overall fitness at first. The system gets more robust, more reliable, but not “better”—until a random event locks it in
