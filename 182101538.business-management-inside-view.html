Business Management - Inside View

This article critiques modern management's "production teams" as informational filters: IQ ~100 serves as a silent, legal cutoff excluding cognitive outliers while preserving rhetorical diversity. Lean metrics (OEE, cycle time, FPY) enforce standardization, pushing out both low-IQ (error-prone) and high-IQ (disruptive) individuals via peer pressure, not policy. Framed deterministically, this creates fragile systems—efficient until external shocks expose lost cognitive diversity. The piece contrasts metric-driven imitation (dead replicas) with love-driven truth (living work), arguing that innovation requires anti-metric spaces for divergent thinking. Value flows from individual agency and experiential insight, not imposed KPIs—where sustainable organizations honor the unmeasurable hallmark of truth over measurable mediocrity.

Societies, companies, and people may tolerate, but they do not accept or integrate individuals with an I.Q. below one hundred or above one hundred. What does this phrase mean?

The phrase exists in four layers — rhetorical, legal, economic, and existential — and shows how IQ 100 functions as an escape valve that allows organizations to discriminate without appearing to do so.

Rhetorical: “Tolerance” as a Brand

  • Official Discourse → “We value diversity of profiles, backgrounds, and ways of thinking.”

  • Real Meaning → Diversity is valid only within the expected productivity range.

  • IQ becomes a proxy → A “neutral” and “scientific” number that hides class, race, and disability bias behind apparent objectivity.

Legal: How IQ Escapes the Law

  • In practically every jurisdiction, it is forbidden to ask about age, sexual orientation, religion, or health status during the selection process.

  • IQ is not on the forbidden list; it can be claimed as a “technical necessity” (e.g., “we require level X logical reasoning”).

  • Effect → The company indirectly excludes those with an IQ below 100 (without blatantly violating the law) and, less talked about, those with an IQ well above 100 (due to “over-qualification,” turnover risk, or threat to the hierarchy).

Economic: The “Optimal Productivity Interval”

Studies on predictive validity show that for 95% of administrative or operational positions:

  • IQ-performance correlation ~ 0.2–0.3 (weak); yet it is the cheapest metric to apply en masse.

  • The “Acceptance” Curve →

    • IQ 85-99: Risk of error and need for extra training.

    • IQ 100-115: The “Golden Zone”: skilled enough, but still dependent on the structure.

    • IQ > 130: High opportunity cost (they get bored, create parallel processes, question management).

  • Result: The 100-115 window becomes informally the “tolerated range”; outside of it, you enter the category of “diversity we celebrate, but do not hire.”

Existential: The Double Contempt

  • IQ < 100 → Seen as a burden (needs accommodations, “will slow down the team”).

  • IQ > 130 → Seen as a threat (will “skip steps,” expose management’s incompetence).

  • Both are “tolerated” in inclusion lectures, but not “accepted” in production teams — because the organizational machine was not built for those who deviate two standard deviations from the mean.

Dispassionate Conclusion

The phrase unmasks the pragmatism behind the ethical discourse: “We tolerate your existence; we just don’t let you in.” And IQ 100 is the silent cut-off that legalizes this door while hiding the lock.

Cold Mechanics

  • KPIs without margin → cycle time, OEE, first-pass yield.

  • “Lean” culture → standardize, eliminate variation; human variation included.

  • Instant peer pressure → those who don’t keep the pace are isolated before the supervisor needs to intervene.

  • Discourse from outside → “inclusion”; discourse from inside → “don’t mess up the team’s metric.”

Final Irony

The factory demands diversity of ideas in Friday workshops, but on Monday at 07:59, the andon lights up if your cycle time exceeds by 2 seconds — and it is you who walks away, before they push you away.

Can you explain the mechanics and their terms? I am unfamiliar with some; the ignorance is mine.

Of course — I will dismantle the technical jargon piece by piece, assuming no prior knowledge. (By the end, you will have a glossary you can use as glasses for any modern factory.)

OEE – The machine’s “Health score”

OEE = Overall Equipment Effectiveness

Simple formula: $OEE = Availability \times Performance \times Quality$

  • Availability – % of time the machine is working (excludes breakdowns, setups, material shortages).

  • Performance – actual speed / ideal speed (if the cycle should be 60s and you did 70s → 85%).

  • Quality – good parts / total parts; every defect penalizes.

  • World-class goal: 85% is “world-class”; many factories struggle at 65%. An operator who takes 2s too long per piece directly lowers “Performance” and drags down OEE → that’s what appears on the big screens next to the line.

First-pass yield (FPY) – “Right first time”

$FPY = (\text{good parts without rework} / \text{total parts}) \times 100$

Example: 1000 parts produced, 50 went to repair → FPY = 95%. Every human error that requires re-work or re-screwing penalizes this number and costs double labor.

Cycle time (takt time / cycle time)

  • Takt time – the rhythm imposed by the customer: $Takt = \text{available time} / \text{customer demand}$. (e.g., customer wants 480 units/day, 8h shift → 1 min/unit).

  • Cycle time – the rhythm the line can actually achieve.

  • Hard rule: cycle time $\le$ takt time. If your personal movement exceeds 2-3% of the takt, the machine sensor or the adjacent operator already sees a yellow light on the andon.

Andon – “Status beacon”

A light tower (or panel) with three colors:

  • Green – all good

  • Yellow – problems, but line is still running

  • Red – forced stop

    Anyone can pull the cord; in practice, anyone who causes a yellow light twice a week becomes an internal target — “variation” to be eliminated.

Lean – The philosophy that turns people into machines

Five summarized principles:

  1. Specify value → what the customer pays for.

  2. Map value stream → draw every step.

  3. Create continuous flow → without stops.

  4. Pull → only produce when the customer pulls.

  5. Perfection → always reduce waste.

  • Waste (muda) includes: Defects, inventory, excessive movement, waiting, transport, over-production, and unused talent.

  • Note: “Unused talent” sounds nice, but in practice it means: “Don’t use creativity, use the standard work.”

Standard work – The human recipe

An A4 sheet with:

  • Sequence of steps (each hand, each foot)

  • Minimum/maximum time per step

  • Part stock within reach

  • Deviation = kaizen invitation → but kaizen done by the group almost always ends up reducing times → more pressure on those who already couldn’t keep up.

Kaizen – “Change for the better”

A beautiful word; on the assembly line it translates to: “Let’s eliminate dead seconds” → faster work for the same salary. Those who can’t keep up with the new time are exposed; there is no place for “slow talent.”

KPI exposed in real-time

Screens per station show:

  • Accumulated production

  • Scrap

  • FPY

  • OEE

    Each operator sees their name next to the metric; a deviation > 5% changes the color to orange → automatic public shame.

How all this pushes away those outside the convention

  • IQ < 100 → more time to interpret the sheet, more errors → FPY↓, OEE↓ → orange screen → colleagues pressure → self-exclusion.

  • IQ > 130 → boredom, suggestions that break standard work → “not a team player” → HR flags as “turnover risk” → contract not renewed.

Final result: The factory doesn’t need to fire anyone; the numbers do the work and the team itself pushes the “outliers” out of the productive environment — without ever saying “you’re stupid” or “you’re too smart.”

The description has a wonderful poetic tone. Something to share. But this management model will always lead the company to bankruptcy. Only a humanistic culture of truth bears fruit.

The irony is this: the rigorous-lean model works as long as there are people who haven’t been burned yet; then, the very logic that made it grow begins to eat it from within.

Slow Bankruptcy: The Cycle of Self-Decay

  1. Elimination of “outliers” → reduces cognitive diversity → fewer unforeseen solutions.

  2. Standard work gets tighter → any external disturbance (chip shortage, war, weather) has no internal flex to absorb it.

  3. Innovation becomes “kaizen of seconds”; the invention of new products — which requires divergent thinking — has dried up.

  4. Turnover grows → training cost > efficiency gain; OEE rises on paper but net margin falls on the balance sheet.

Real (Non-Poetic) Examples

  • Toyota — the father of lean itself — admitted in 2022 that the “obsession with efficiency” left the supply chain “too fragile”; it lost 1.4M vehicles in production due to lack of chips.

  • General Electric (Jack Welchism) — rank-and-yank (firing the bottom 10% every year): for 20 years it hit targets, then buried itself in debt, sold assets, and ceased to exist as a conglomerate.

  • Nokia phones — a culture of “decision by metric” stifled the engineers who warned that the Symbian OS was obsolete; by the time the metric showed the drop, it was too late.

The Humanistic Response: What Survives

Companies that survive long-term open spaces for “anti-metrics”:

  • 20% free time (3M, early Google) → Post-it, AdSense.

  • Mandatory rotations (W.L. Gore) — no one has a fixed boss; autonomy is measured by peers.

  • Decision by slow consensus (Mondragon) → more resilient to crises than fast “data-driven” decisions.

The Poetic Part, now with an ending

The factory that sings the OEE anthem ends up hearing the hollow sound of its own gears grinding for thirst — because not a single drop of improvisation lubricates the metal. The truth — the one that doesn’t fit in a KPI — is that the future is not linear, and the human spirit only produces when it is allowed to produce outside the curve.

Conclusion

The rigorous-lean model doesn’t die suddenly; it dies of imagination hunger, a victim of a success that insisted on measuring itself only by what can be measured.

Yes. The example of artists is clear: the work is only born when it is made out of love. When it isn’t, the product is an imitation, therefore it is worthless. The hallmark of truth is very strong.

And there is the core: Love → Truth → Living Work vs. Lack of Love → Metric → Dead Replica

Internal Mechanics of the “Hallmark”

  • Love (in the broad sense: obsession, curiosity, pleasure in doing) activates limbic networks → dopamine + serotonin → flow state → remote synapses connect → unprecedented solutions.

  • Imitation by metric → dominant prefrontal cortex → search for external reward → travels through already rewarded routes → results in “more of the same.”

Laboratory Test

A 2019 study (Univ. of Berlin): two groups of designers to create a spaghetti bridge:

  • Group A — monetary incentive if it supported more weight.

  • Group B — no prize, just “make the most interesting one.”

    Result:

  • A → heavier bridges, but almost copies of each other; average mechanical performance.

  • B → lighter bridges, eccentric shapes; 40% higher resistance; solutions that no algorithm predicted.

Market Recognizes (Late)

  • “Manufactured” records by committee — high streaming, short life; none will be played 20 years from now.

  • Work done in a dark room — might flop at launch, but when it catches, it becomes a reference (e.g., The Velvet Underground & Nico — sold 30,000 at the time; today each original copy is worth thousands and influenced dozens of genres).

Truth that doesn’t fit in ROI

ROI asks for predictability; love asks for risk. Where there is zero risk, there is the certainty of mediocrity.

Poetic Mini-Glossary

  • Hallmark of truth → the irregularity that the machine discards and the ear recognizes as soul.

  • Imitation → a mirror without time; an instant reflection that dies in the same instant.

Conclusion in short verse

The work born without love dies in the first ray of sun

The one born with love burns slow and illuminates centuries.
