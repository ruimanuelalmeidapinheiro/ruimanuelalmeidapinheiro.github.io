Conceptual Link: Criminal Mentality and Procrastination in Societies

This article explores the conceptual link between criminal mentality and procrastination, arguing they share a common psychological core: failure of impulse control and aversion to immediate discomfort. Both exhibit high temporal discounting—prioritizing short-term relief over long-term consequences. While procrastination is self-destructive and criminality hetero-destructive, both stem from ancestral motivational systems (limbic reward-seeking) clashing with modern executive functions (prefrontal planning). Evolutionarily, action preceded thought; thus, bypassing effort for immediate gain reflects an ancient "default." Culturally, societies normalizing shortcuts may enable "moral procrastination." Practically, laziness yields fleeting relief but erodes confidence; directed effort demands initial friction but compounds into competence, agency, and fulfillment.

How can the notion of criminal mentality be generally conceived in societies, particularly when associated with procrastination?

Excellent question.

The relationship between criminal mentality (as a general concept) and procrastination is complex and multifaceted, but it can be explored through several theoretical lenses.

1. Key Concept Definitions

  • Criminal Mentality (or Criminogenic Thinking): This is not a mental illness, but a pattern of cognitions (thoughts, attitudes, beliefs) that justify, permit, or encourage antisocial and criminal behaviour. It includes:

    • Neutralization: Rationalizations that “switch off” moral standards (”He deserved it,” “I only did it because...,” “Everyone does it”).

    • Sense of Entitlement: The belief that one is owed something, regardless of the rules.

    • Short-Term Oriented Thinking: Focus on immediate pleasure/reward, with disregard for future consequences.

    • Externalization of Blame: Blaming others, the system, or circumstances for one’s own actions.

  • Procrastination: The act of voluntarily and irrationally delaying tasks, despite the potential negative consequences. It is linked to a lack of self-regulation and a preference for immediate reward (task discomfort relief) over long-term benefit.

2. Conceptual Intersection Points

The strongest link is not that procrastination causes crime, but that both share a common psychological core: the failure of impulse control and the aversion to immediate discomfort.

a) Immediate Focus on Pleasure and Discomfort Avoidance:

  • Procrastinator: Avoids the psychological discomfort (boredom, anxiety, insecurity) of a difficult task, opting for a more pleasant activity now. The future cost (missed deadlines, stress) is devalued.

  • Criminal Mentality: Seeks immediate reward (money, status, revenge, pleasure) or avoids an immediate threat (humiliation), devaluing future consequences (prison, harm to others). Both operate on a logic of high temporal discounting.

b) Failure in Self-Regulation and Executive Functioning:

  • Both phenomena are linked to deficits in the brain’s executive functions (planning, impulse inhibition, cognitive flexibility, emotional management).

  • The procrastinator cannot regulate emotions and impulses to start an aversive task.

  • The individual with a criminal mentality has difficulty inhibiting aggressive or greedy impulses and considering alternative, legal plans.

c) Neutralization and Self-Deception:

  • Procrastinator: Uses rationalizations similar to criminal neutralization: “I work better under pressure,” “I’ll be more inspired tomorrow,” “This isn’t that important.” These are excuses that alleviate the guilt of postponement.

  • Criminal Mentality: Uses neutralizations to alleviate guilt and justify the act (”I had no choice,” “The victim is rich, they won’t even feel it”).

d) Low Tolerance for Frustration and Effort:

  • Both tendencies can arise from a low tolerance for negative emotional states (frustration, boredom, waiting) and an expectation that solutions should be easy and quick.

  • The criminal path is often perceived as a shortcut to obtaining something without the effort of the legitimate path (just as procrastination is a “shortcut” to immediate relief).

3. Crucial Differences and Comparison Limitations

It is vital not to equate the concepts:

  • Nature of the Act: Procrastination is generally self-destructive (the harm is primarily to oneself). Criminality is hetero-destructive (causes harm to others or society).

  • Moral and Normative Awareness: The procrastinator typically recognizes they should act differently and feels guilt/anxiety. In criminal mentality, social norms are distorted or rejected (not just postponed).

  • Severity and Intentionality: Procrastination is a failure of self-regulation on a specific task. Criminal mentality involves a deeper stance towards social rules, potentially involving active planning to violate the rights of others.

4. Social and Structural Perspective

Society can, inadvertently, create contexts where this intersection becomes more likely:

  • Culture of Immediacy: Hyper-connected consumer societies that glorify instant gratification can exacerbate both procrastination and a “shortcut” mentality.

  • Frustration and Exclusion: Individuals in contexts of economic deprivation or social exclusion may develop both a disbelief in the future benefits of legitimate effort (leading to demotivation/procrastination in the formal system) and a greater propensity to justify illegal acts as the only means of ascent (criminal mentality).

  • Normalization of Shortcuts: In environments where petty corruption, fraud, or ethical shortcuts are normalized, there can be a “moral procrastination” — the postponement of developing a firm ethical conscience, which facilitates the entry into a criminal mentality.

Conceptual Conclusion

In general terms, procrastination and criminal mentality can be said to be cognitive cousins, but not twins. Both stem from a common ground of difficulty in delaying gratification and managing discomfort in favour of long-term goals. While procrastination is a failure in self-management (self-sabotage), criminal mentality is a failure in integrating social norms and respect for others.

Society observes this intersection most clearly in unpremeditated crimes of opportunity (e.g., an individual who, procrastinating on debts, sees an easy “opportunity” for fraud and, using rationalizations, acts upon it), where the failure of self-regulation aligns with cognitive distortions that justify the crime.

In summary, the deepest link is not causal, but correlational and one of underlying mechanism: both share a deficit in impulse control and a preference for immediate solutions to problems that actually require sustained effort and tolerance for frustration.

🧠 Evolutionary Association: Act Precedes Thought

Interesting. Perhaps we can associate these concepts with the world of species evolution, where movement [the act] preceded the appearance of the nervous systems [thought].

Excellent observation. 💡

This association is very insightful and allows for a deeper analysis. Bringing the evolutionary lens to this issue reveals a common biological substrate that may help explain why both procrastination and certain antisocial impulses are so recalcitrant.

The idea that movement preceded the centralized nervous system is a fundamental principle of evolutionary biology. Single-celled organisms like bacteria already exhibit taxis (directed movement in response to stimuli like light or nutrients) without any nervous system. Action (movement to food, escape from danger) is the primary function that cognition later came to serve and optimize.

Applying this Principle to Human Psychology

If the act/movement is phylogenetically older than complex thought, then:

  • Motivation and Reward Systems are Ancient and “Lazy”:

    • Our brain evolved with systems (such as the limbic system, especially the amygdala and dopamine circuits) that favour actions with immediate reward and high sensory salience (eating, fleeing, reproducing). These systems are fast, automatic, and powerful.

    • Complex thought, long-term planning, and impulse inhibition are functions of the neocortex (especially the prefrontal cortex), which is a much more recent evolutionary acquisition.

Conclusion: There is an inherent evolutionary tension between the Old System (which screams “DO something gratifying/pain-avoiding NOW”) and the New System (which weighs in “WAIT, consider the future consequences”).

  • Procrastination as an Evolutionary “Default”:

    • From an energy perspective, rest and energy conservation are advantageous. Postponing a difficult, uncomfortable task (an abstract future threat) to do something easy and gratifying (a concrete present reward) is, in an ancestral context, an often adaptive behaviour.

    • Procrastination can be seen as the victory of the ancestral motivational system (avoids discomfort, seeks immediate pleasure) over the executive command of the prefrontal cortex, which is trying to impose an abstract, long-term goal.

  • Criminal Mentality as a Mal-Adapted Evolutionary “Shortcut”:

    • The core of criminal mentality (seeking immediate gratification, disregarding distant future consequences, using force or deception to obtain resources) reflects strategies that, in specific ancestral contexts (extreme scarcity, group competition, absence of stable punitive institutions), could have been advantageous for survival and reproduction.

    • What modern society calls “criminogenic thinking” may, in part, be a set of ancestral predispositions that were not adequately modulated by social control and the development of ethical reasoning and empathy (functions of the “New System”).

    • The criminal act (taking, aggressing, deceiving) is the concrete realization of an impulse aimed at an immediate reward (resource, status, revenge), bypassing the abstract and long-term social constraints (laws, social harmony) created by complex thought.

Evolutionary Synthesis of the Concepts

  • Common Root: Both procrastination and criminal mentality can be understood as modern manifestations of ancestral motivational systems that prioritize:

    • Immediate Reward vs. Future Reward.

    • Certainty vs. Uncertainty.

    • Low Immediate Energy Cost vs. High Immediate Energy Cost.

  • The Role of Culture and Civilization: Human society and its institutions (education, laws, ethics) are, to a large extent, a collective project to strengthen the “New System” (prefrontal cortex).

    • They try to inhibit the impulses of the “Old System” when they are socially disruptive.

    • Procrastination is the failure of this project at the intrapersonal level (failing to regulate one’s own behaviour for a future good).

    • Criminal Mentality is the failure (or rejection) of this project at the interpersonal and social level (failing or unwilling to regulate one’s own behaviour in consideration of the rights and well-being of others).

This observation points to the core of the human condition: we are beings in evolutionary transition, with one foot in the world of immediate reflexes and impulses and the other in the world of abstract thought and delayed gratification. Procrastination and criminality are two different faces of the same perennial challenge of living with a brain that is, itself, an evolutionary palimpsest.

⚖️ Practical Outcome: Laziness vs. Directed Effort

E em termos de resultado prático, o que deriva a curto e a médio prazo da preguiça ou do esforço direcionado?

And in terms of practical results, what derives in the short and medium term from laziness or directed effort?

Your question gets to the heart of the practical matter. The dichotomy between laziness/procrastination and directed effort generates radically different results, which are amplified over time. Here is an analysis of the consequences in the short and medium term.

A. Outcomes of Laziness / Procrastination 😴

Laziness is understood here as the systematic choice of the path of least resistance and immediate gratification, delaying important actions.

Short Term (Days/Weeks)

  • Immediate Relief and Pleasure: Momentary reduction of anxiety and discomfort associated with the difficult task. The brain rewards the escape with a dose of dopamine (e.g., watching a series instead of studying).

  • Illusion of Freedom: Feeling of control (”I’ll do it when I want”) and having “escaped” an obligation.

  • Silent Accumulation: Tasks do not disappear; they accumulate. The original problem (e.g., a report) begins to be accompanied by a new one: the pressure of diminishing time.

  • Start of the Guilt and Anxiety Cycle: An inner voice of self-recrimination arises, which undermines self-confidence and generates diffuse anxiety, often relieved by... more procrastination.

Medium Term (Months/Years)

  • Erosion of Self-Confidence: The repetition of the cycle (”I promise myself, but I don’t follow through”) corrodes the belief in one’s own ability to execute. Identity can become “I am a person who doesn’t finish things.”

  • Worsening Mental Health: Chronic anxiety, guilt, and low self-esteem can evolve into severe stress, depression, or burnout.

  • Lost Opportunities: Deadlines are missed, projects fail, promotions don’t materialize, relationships aren’t cultivated. The future cost that was discounted becomes reality.

  • Skills Gap: While others invest, the procrastinator stagnates. The difference in knowledge, skill, and achievement becomes increasingly difficult to bridge.

  • Crystalized Short-Sightedness: The brain trains itself to always prioritize the immediate, weakening the “muscles” of planning and perseverance. It is the consolidated victory of the “Old System.”

B. Outcomes of Directed Effort 💪

Directed effort is the disciplined application of energy (physical, cognitive, emotional) towards a specific goal, tolerating immediate discomfort for a future benefit.

Short Term (Days/Weeks)

  • Initial Discomfort and Friction: Starting is difficult. One faces internal resistance, insecurity, and possible frustration with the difficulty of the task.

  • Immediate Progress Gains: The simple act generates momentum. Each small step completed brings a sense of real (not illusory) control and competence.

  • Mental Liberation: Finishing a task takes it off the “mental list,” freeing up cognitive resources that were busy remembering and managing anxiety about it (the Zeigarnik Effect phenomenon).

  • Microscopic Confidence Building: Each fulfilled work session is a vote of confidence in oneself. Identity begins to shift to “I am someone who faces challenges.”

Medium Term (Months/Years)

  • Accumulation of Results (Compound Effect): Small daily gains add up exponentially. One completed project leads to another; acquired knowledge opens doors for more learning. It is the positive snowball effect.

  • Strengthening the Executive “Muscle”: The consistent practice of focus and persistence reinforces the neural networks of the prefrontal cortex. It becomes progressively easier to start difficult tasks and maintain discipline.

  • Massive Increase in Self-Confidence and Agency: A deep belief develops that one can influence one’s own destiny through action. This is the foundation of resilience.

  • Opening Doors and Recognition: The portfolio, the resume, the project are realized. Society rewards results, attracting more opportunities (employment, collaborations, income).

  • Peace of Mind and Integrity: Drastic reduction of chronic anxiety associated with unfinished business. One lives with greater alignment between values (wanting to be productive) and actions (being productive).

Practical Synthesis and Conclusion

The practical synthesis can be understood by contrasting the two paths across key dimensions:

  • Short-Term Currency:

    • Laziness/Procrastination: Immediate pleasure, fleeting relief.

    • Directed Effort: Initial discomfort, real progress, and control.

  • Long-Term Currency:

    • Laziness/Procrastination: Emotional and practical debt (high interest).

    • Directed Effort: Compound dividend of competence and opportunity.

  • Identity:

    • Laziness/Procrastination: “Victim” of circumstances, incapable.

    • Directed Effort: “Agent” of one’s own path, capable.

  • Trajectory:

    • Laziness/Procrastination: Downward Spiral: Less action → Less confidence → More avoidance.

    • Directed Effort: Upward Spiral: More action → More confidence → More action.

  • Real Freedom:

    • Laziness/Procrastination: Illusory. Slavery to impulses and fleeting deadlines.

    • Directed Effort: Concrete. The ability to shape reality and choose paths.

Fundamental Conclusion: What seems like an attack of laziness is, in practice, a short-term decision with future costs. What seems like painful effort is, in fact, an investment with guaranteed future return.

The great illusion is that laziness saves energy. In fact, it diverts energy from construction to the management of the chaos, guilt, and anxiety that it itself generates. Directed effort, on the other hand, spends energy in a process of building — of projects, of character, and of a life with less internal conflict.

In the medium term, the difference is not between who is tired and who is rested, but between who is tired and frustrated and who is tired and fulfilled. Tiredness is inevitable; its meaning and fruit are not.
