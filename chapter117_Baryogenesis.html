Who: Andrei Sakharov

Andrei Dmitrievich Sakharov (1921–1989) was a Soviet theoretical physicist, cosmologist, and human rights activist. He is considered the father of the Soviet hydrogen bomb, but later became a vocal dissident and advocate for human rights, winning the 1975 Nobel Peace Prize.

In 1967, Sakharov published a groundbreaking paper proposing the conditions necessary for baryogenesis — the creation of the baryon asymmetry of the universe. He realized that the universe's matter dominance could be explained by three conditions:

  1. Baryon number violation — processes that change the number of baryons
  2. C and CP violation — matter and antimatter behave differently
  3. Departure from thermal equilibrium — the universe must be out of equilibrium

Sakharov was a brilliant physicist and a courageous human being. He was exiled to Gorky (now Nizhny Novgorod) for his activism but continued his scientific work in isolation. His legacy is both scientific and moral.

Baryogenesis: Creating the Phase Imbalance

In the Wave Ontology framework, baryogenesis is the creation of a phase imbalance between $f>0$ (matter) and $f<0$ (antimatter) phase modes. The universe starts with equal $f>0$ and $f<0$ modes. Through the Sakharov conditions, a slight phase imbalance is created:

$$ \eta = \frac{n_B - n_{\bar{B}}}{n_\gamma} \approx 6 \times 10^{-10} $$

This tiny imbalance — about one part in ten billion — is the reason we exist. If there were no baryogenesis, the universe would be a symmetric sea of matter and antimatter, with no atoms, no stars, no life.

Electroweak Baryogenesis: One possible mechanism for baryogenesis is electroweak baryogenesis at the electroweak phase transition. During the electroweak phase transition (at $T \sim 100$ GeV), the SU(2) × U(1) symmetry is broken. CP violation in the weak interaction, combined with baryon number violation (sphalerons) and non-equilibrium conditions, could create the baryon asymmetry. However, the Standard Model's CP violation is too small to explain the observed asymmetry — requiring new physics beyond the Standard Model.
