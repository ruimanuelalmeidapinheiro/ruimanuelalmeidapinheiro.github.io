Carl Gustav Jung’s archetypes are universal, inherited patterns of thought and behavior that reside in the collective unconscious—a shared psychic layer all humans inherit, distinct from personal experiences.

Core Archetypes

The Self The unified center of the psyche, representing wholeness and the integration of conscious and unconscious elements. Often symbolized by mandalas, circles, or sacred geometry.

The Shadow Everything the ego rejects or finds unacceptable—repressed desires, weaknesses, instincts, and “negative” traits. Not inherently evil; integrating the shadow is essential for psychological growth.

The Anima/Animus The feminine aspect within men (anima) and masculine aspect within women (animus). Bridges the conscious mind and the unconscious, often appearing in dreams as opposite-gender figures.

The Persona The social mask we wear to fit in—the “public face” that conceals the true self. Necessary for social function but dangerous if confused with one’s authentic identity.

Other Major Archetypes

How They Work

Archetypes aren’t fixed images—they’re dynamic potentials that activate when life situations trigger them. They appear in:

  • Dreams and fantasies

  • Myths and religions worldwide

  • Art and literature

  • Projections onto other people

Jung saw individuation (becoming your true self) as the process of consciously engaging these archetypes rather than being blindly controlled by them.
