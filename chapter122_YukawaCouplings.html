Introduction: The Yukawa Couplings — Phase-Locking Fermions to the Higgs

The Yukawa couplings are the fifth term of the Standard Model Lagrangian. They describe how fermions acquire mass through their interaction with the Higgs field. The term is:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{Yukawa}} = -y_{ij} \bar{\psi}_{L,i} \phi \psi_{R,j} + \text{h.c.} $$

This term is the phase-locking term between fermion modes and the Higgs scalar phase field. The Yukawa coupling $y_{ij}$ is a matrix — it describes the strength of phase-locking between different fermion flavors and the Higgs field. When the Higgs field acquires its VEV ($\langle \phi \rangle = v/\sqrt{2}$), the Yukawa couplings become fermion masses:

$$ m_f = \frac{y_f v}{\sqrt{2}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_f = \frac{y_f v}{\sqrt{2}} \frac{c^2}{h} $$

The Yukawa couplings are the origin of fermion masses in the Standard Model. They also encode the flavor mixing between generations — the CKM matrix for quarks and the PMNS matrix for leptons.

Who: Hideki Yukawa — The Pioneer of Meson Theory

Hideki Yukawa (1907–1981) was a Japanese theoretical physicist at Kyoto University and the University of Tokyo. In 1935, he proposed the Yukawa potential — a theory of the strong force mediated by massive particles (mesons). He predicted the existence of the pion, which was discovered in 1947. Yukawa was awarded the 1949 Nobel Prize in Physics "for his prediction of the existence of mesons on the basis of theoretical work on nuclear forces."

The Yukawa interaction in the Standard Model is named after him, although the modern Yukawa term describes fermion-Higgs interactions rather than the strong force. The Yukawa coupling constant $y$ is the strength of the interaction between fermions and the Higgs field.

The Yukawa matrix for quarks was extended by Kobayashi and Maskawa (1973) to include three generations, explaining CP violation. The Yukawa matrix for leptons describes neutrino masses and mixing.
