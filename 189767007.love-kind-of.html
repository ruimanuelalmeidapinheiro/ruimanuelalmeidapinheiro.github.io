Here’s a detailed explanation of both concepts and where the perceived connection comes from.

1. Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs (The Psychological Model)

This is a well-established psychological theory proposed by Abraham Maslow in 1943.

  • What it is: A motivational theory depicting human needs as a five-tier pyramid (sometimes expanded to six or more tiers). The idea is that you must reasonably satisfy the needs at the lower levels before you can progress to addressing the higher ones.

  • The Tiers (from bottom to top): Physiological Needs: Air, water, food, shelter, sleep, clothing. This is the foundation. Safety Needs: Personal security, employment, resources, health, property. Love and Belonging: Friendship, intimacy, family, sense of connection. Esteem Needs: Respect, self-esteem, status, recognition, strength, freedom. Self-Actualization: The desire to become the most that one can be; realizing personal potential.

This is a model of human motivation and growth, not a physical measurement.

2. Frequency of Vibrations in the Human Body (The Biophysical/Metaphysical Concept)

This idea comes from two main areas: a loose interpretation of physics and energy-based spiritual traditions.

  • The Scientific Angle: At a quantum level, all matter is made of atoms, which are in constant motion and therefore have a measurable vibrational frequency. However, these frequencies are extremely high and not directly correlated with emotional or spiritual states in a way that mainstream science recognizes.

  • The Metaphysical/Wellness Angle: This perspective, popular in New Age and holistic practices, posits that our body, organs, and especially our “energy fields” or “chakras” vibrate at specific frequencies. In this view: Lower vibrations are associated with negative emotions like fear, anger, and shame. Higher vibrations are associated with positive states like love, joy, peace, and enlightenment. The goal of many spiritual practices (like meditation, yoga, sound healing) is to “raise your vibration.”

This concept is not empirically measurable with current scientific instruments and is considered a metaphor or a subjective experience.

💡 The Deeper Philosophical Divide

Whitehead’s objection to Einstein was not merely technical but profoundly philosophical. He accused Einstein of committing a “fallacy of misplaced concreteness“—the error of mistaking an abstract scientific model for concrete reality itself.

For Whitehead, Einstein’s theory made the entire structure of geometry dependent on the accidental distribution of matter. He argued this created a vicious circle: you must know the entire universe’s mass distribution to know its geometry, but you need to know the geometry to measure the mass distribution. Furthermore, he believed Einstein’s theory dismissed our direct experience of a flowing, irreversible time as a mere “stubbornly persistent illusion”.

Whitehead’s alternative was a process philosophy, where reality is fundamentally composed of interconnected events (”actual occasions”) rather than isolated material things. His theory of relativity was an attempt to build a physics compatible with this worldview, one that took our lived experience of time, simultaneity, and a coherent world seriously.

🔭 Scientific Status and Modern Relevance

As a physical theory, Whitehead’s model is a compelling alternative that should not be dismissed.

  • For a time, it was empirically equivalent to Einstein’s for the classic tests of relativity.

  • However, as more precise cosmological observations emerged (like the orbits of binary pulsars), Einstein’s General Relativity proved more accurate.

  • Some physicists argue that ad-hoc introductions like “dark matter” and “dark energy” to save Einstein’s model are modern-day “epicycles,” a criticism that aligns with Whitehead’s philosophical worry about forcing nature to fit a model.

While Whitehead’s specific physical theory has been superseded, his philosophical critique remains powerfully relevant. His insistence that science must account for human experience and avoid the pitfalls of “model-centrism” resonates in ongoing debates about the interpretation of quantum mechanics and the nature of time.
