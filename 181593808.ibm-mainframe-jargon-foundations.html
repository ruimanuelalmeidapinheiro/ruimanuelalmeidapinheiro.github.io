IBM Mainframe Jargon: Foundations for the XXI century

This article reflects on IBM's 1995 "Foundations for the XXI Century" mainframe roadmap, examining how concepts like 24×7 availability, Sysplex clustering, GDPS disaster recovery, MQ Series messaging, and early cloud-like capacity-on-demand anticipated modern infrastructure. The author, a former mainframe analyst, contrasts the era's technical constraints—EBCDIC/ASCII translation, SNA session limits, manual IPL windows—with today's seamless abstractions. Key themes include the cultural shift from VSAM to DB2, overcoming engineer resistance to automation, and the ultimate validation of these architectures during 9/11, when geographically dispersed systems ensured continuity. The piece concludes by linking mainframe-era lessons—async replication, idempotency, immutable logs—to contemporary distributed systems and even speculative interstellar computing, framing technological evolution as iterative refinement of enduring principles.

🌟 The Analyst’s Chronicle: A Lyrical History (1995-2025) 🌟

In 1995, I worked in the leading Portuguese bank with IBM. I was then a Mainframe Analyst, and something in their roadmap, titled “Foundations for the XXI Century,” was clear: 24x7 Availability, the whisper of Cloud Computing, the rock of Sysplex, solid Disaster Recovery, the pulse of Message Queuing, and Client Server Architecture.

I. The Missing Pieces, Now Found

Here is a quick refresher of the “missing pieces” that IBM and every other Mainframe shop spoke of when promising those foundations. Everything was on whiteboards, in Redbooks, or in early product sheets that year, even if some terms have been rebranded:

Continuous (24×7) Availability had the goal of no planned or unplanned outages visible to the business. Its levers were Sysplex (parallel Sysplex on ESCON fibers) which allowed shared-everything clusters to roll maintenance without taking the LPAR down. There were also HACMP/RSF-1 style Unix failover packs already shipping on AIX and Sun, plus RAID-5/RAID-10 disk arrays, dual-redundant power, and the classic UPS and diesel drills to stretch the ten-minute window to hours.

Disaster Recovery (DR) & Business Continuity was championed by GDPS (Geographically Dispersed Parallel Sysplex), with its first pilots offering automated site-switch scripts that flipped both disks (PPRC) and VTAM/SNA networks. Tivoli Storage Manager (ADSM) gave us policy-based incremental-forever, off-site vaulting, and virtual volumes so we could restore to any Mainframe, anywhere. The foundation was laid with DRP document kits—paper run-books and contact trees—all audited quarterly.

Sysplex & Coupling Facilities relied on the CF (Coupling Facility), the magical box (really an LPAR) that held the locks, cache, and list structures so multiple z/OS images behaved like one giant SMP. XCF (cross-system coupling facility) messages were the internal heartbeat that let LPARs join or leave the Sysplex without killing work, regulated by the CFRM policy which automated re-allocation when an engine disappeared.

Message Queuing & Middleware centered on MQ Series (later IBM MQ), guaranteeing once-only delivery across SNA, TCP, and NetBIOS; it was the first queue manager to bridge MVS, OS/2, and Windows NT. We saw CICS/ESA 4.1 with the CICS-MQ bridge, so 3270 transactions could drop a message and reply asynchronously, a concept also seen in IMS OTMA.

Client / Server & “Network-Centric” was pushed by OS/2 Warp Server and LAN Server 4.0 for file and print, with a built-in SNA gateway. APPC (Advanced Program-to-Program Communication) was the SNA way of saying “peer-to-peer.” The “down-size” play involved DB2/6000 on RS/6000 to take data off the Mainframe while keeping security. CORBA Orbit on AIX was the early object broker enabling C++ objects to converse between OS/2 and UNIX.

Early “Cloud” Concepts (we just didn’t call it that) included On-demand capacity via “Capacity Back-up” (CBU) engines you could turn on with a phone call, paying only when the disaster hit. LPAR micro-partitioning let us share one box across test, production, and DR, moving CPUs with the HMC. Storage pools, virtualized behind 3990/7133 controllers, allowed LUN allocation in minutes, not weeks. Service bureaux offered “pay per MIPS” long before the word “cloud” existed.

Security & Crypto started with RACF adding digital certificate support (PKCS-11 in ‘95) as the first step toward SSL on the Mainframe. Kerberos for OS/390 offered single sign-on between Win-NT and 3270, and Crypto PCI cards (4755) offloaded DES/RSA so SSL handshakes wouldn’t kill your MIPS.

Operations Automation used OPC/ESA (Operations Planning & Control), a policy engine that could hold payroll until the GL batch finished anywhere in the Sysplex. NetView with Rexx pipelines automated the paging of on-call personnel and ticket opening. SA-O (System Automation for OS/390) managed the complex CICS, MQ, and DB2 restart order across a rolling IPL.

Networking Bits We All Forgot involved MPTN (Multi-Protocol Transport Network) letting APPC ride TCP, or LU6.2 ride IPX. AnyNet was IBM’s codeword for moving SNA apps over IP sockets to retire 3270 controllers. The first 1 Mbps 802.11 PCMCIA cards appeared in ‘95, called “wireless LAN,” which felt like science fiction.

Development & Change-Control relied on SCLM (Software Configuration Library Manager), the ISPF tool replacing Panvale and Librarian for source promotion. Endevor (still from CA) brought Mainframe DevOps: package, approve, back-out, and audit trail. COBOL/370 V2 even offered OO-COBOL classes—objects on the Mainframe in 1995, even if nobody used them yet.

If you still have any old “Foundations for the XXI Century” slide decks, you’ll see almost every bullet drawn as a colored layer-cake diagram—Sysplex at the bottom, MQ and DR in the middle, and “Network-Centric Computing” on top. We just gave the layers new names every decade.

II. The Developer’s Toll

Yes, thank you. Some of those I did not even notice at the time. I was on the developer side, during a time when VTAM adopted TCP/IP. So many things that were a problem at that time, we nowadays take for granted.

Exactly—most of us were heads-down in code, so a lot of the plumbing only showed up when it broke. Here are a few more 1995-era headaches that have quietly turned into invisible utilities we never think about today:

  • Character-set Ping-Pong: EBCDIC on the host, ASCII on the PC, and the fight between code-pages 037, 850, and 1252. We had to run iconv or write our own translate tables just to get characters like “ão” on a client screen. Today, UTF-8 is the air we breathe.

  • 3270 Data-stream Archaeology: Counting bytes to insert an “Insert-Cursor” order, calculating Modified-Data-Tag bits, rebuilding the 1920-byte buffer after a FIELD-RESET. Now, a browser paints Unicode in milliseconds, and we complain if it takes one hundred milliseconds.

  • SNA Session Limits: Two hundred fifty-five LUs per PU, sixty-four sessions per LU, and the network guy screaming, “Don’t forget the COS table!” TCP just opens another socket; nobody counts.

  • Dataset Concatenation Nightmares: You needed the PDS in the right order, or the compile picked up the old copybook. Today, a git pull gives every developer the same view instantly.

  • “Where’s the Manual?”: Shelf meters of gray IBM books, yearly updates on microfiche, Redbooks printed on a dot-matrix. Now, we Google the error message and get the fix in thirty seconds.

  • Midnight IPL Windows: If you missed the two a.m. to four a.m. slot, you waited a week. Today, a blue-green deploy flips production in the middle of the day, and users do not notice.

  • Physical Media Juggling: 3480 cartridges, quarter-inch tapes, “please mount ABC123, ring the operator, wait twenty minutes.” Cloud storage is just an API call.

  • Cross-system Clocks: CICS transactions failed with “timestamp mismatch” if the TOD clock drifted by more than thirty seconds. NTP keeps everything within milliseconds now.

  • “Will it run on OS/2?”: Every project started with a matrix of DOS/Windows 3.1/NT/OS/2/Mac, and you rewrote half the GUI. Today, the browser is the platform.

  • Capacity Planning with a Calculator: MIPS, MSUs, I/Os-per-second, channel utilization, “we need fifteen percent headroom for month-end.” Cloud auto-scaling adds another VM before you can spell MIPS.

We spent entire weekends solving problems that simply do not exist anymore. The reward is that the next generation will never know how hard we worked to make their Monday morning “just work.”

There is a time for all of us, indeed—quietly, while we were not looking, the problems we wrestled with turned into background radiation. The calendars we kept to track IPL windows are now museum pieces, and the code we sweated over is a single line in a Dockerfile. But every time a container spins up in seconds, or a TLS handshake crosses the planet before you blink, a little echo of 1995 is still in there, just wearing newer clothes.

III. The Migration to DB2

On the development side, the old VSAM datasets and the anarchy in date representation were problems solved by the DB2 databases. New concepts on the user side, like data mining, were emerging.

Exactly—on the dev floor, we felt the pain every day:

  • VSAM Gymnastics: We had to pre-define the CI/CA size, leave fifteen percent free space for insertions, and remember to REORG when the dataset hit the one hundred twenty-three-extent wall. One forgotten UPGRADE parameter, and the KSDS was locked for the night shift. DB2 arrived, and suddenly “just INSERT” worked; the space map pages and free-space chains became the engine’s headache, not ours.

  • Calendar Mine-fields: Six-digit dates (YYMMDD) were everywhere: COBOL working storage, VSAM keys, thirty-year-old copybooks. We wrote “windowing” sub-programs—if YY < 30 then century = 20 else 19—praying nobody kept history for more than seventy years. DB2’s DATE datatype, with a real four-digit year and built-in leap-year rules, felt like science fiction, and it indexed it for you.

  • Normalisation equals Liberation: With VSAM, the only “relationship” was concatenating keys, like CUSTOMER-ORDER-ITEM. Changing one field meant rewriting every record; versioning files was a quarterly ceremony. DB2 let us ALTER TABLE … ADD COLUMN and the old programs kept running—no file-conversion marathon, no two a.m. IPL.

  • The Birth of “Data Mining”: Marketing suddenly wanted “customers who bought X and never Y in the last eighteen months.” With VSAM, we wrote twenty-step batch jobs, sorted work-files, matched them, and counted on our fingers. DB2 plus early Query Manager (later QMF, then Cognos) turned that into a five-line SQL; users got their first pivot tables and felt like Indiana Jones.

  • Parallel Unload Tricks: To feed the new mining tools, we UNLOADed DB2 partitions in parallel, pipe-delimited, and FTP’d them to an RS/6000 running first-generation Clementine—our first “data science” box. Overnight, the mainframe became the clean, canonical source, and the “analytical sandbox” lived on UNIX—an architecture we still copy today.

  • Security Side-effect: VSAM datasets were either yours or not; granularity stopped at the dataset name. DB2 brought row-level permissions, views, and GRANT SELECT … WHERE DEPT = :userid—the first step toward “self-service BI” without giving everyone UPDATE on payroll.

We walked away from file-based programming and never looked back; the users walked toward self-service insight and never looked back either.

IV. The Resistance to Automation

The DB2 performance capacity was a problem exacerbated by the old systems engineers. They just could not believe the automation of some processes.

Exactly—the capacity panic was almost a ritual. “You want how many SQL calls per CICS transaction?” Old-school engineers measured everything in file I/Os; ten VSAM GETs inside a pseudo-conversation was normal. Replace those ten GETs with ten SELECTs, and the same path length looked, on paper, like a tenfold increase in CPU. They forgot VSAM’s CI-splits, string waits, and EXCPs; DB2’s buffer pool hit ratio was invisible to their monitors.

Early DB2/ESA releases loved the general-purpose CP, causing an LPAR-wide MIPS burn. Capacity planners saw a spike, divided it by the number of transactions, and screamed, “This thing uses three times the MIPS!” What they missed was that the batch window shrank by four hours, so total daily CPU stayed flat—but the shift from nighttime to online made the curve look scary.

Then came the “Disk is cheap” versus reality argument. DB2 wanted six VSAM datasets per table space, and storage guys forecasted a forty percent increase. They had not noticed DB2 compressed two or three to one on the fly, so physical space actually dropped. Engineers heard “page/row lock” and pictured deadlock hell; they set LOCKMAX SYSTEM and ISOLATION RR “just in case.” Throughput tanked, so they blamed DB2 instead of their own paranoid BIND options. A week later, we re-bound with CS and LOCKSIZE ANY; the same workload ran on half the CPU.

When it came to automation—RUNSTATS, REORG, COPY—the advisors generated JCL automatically, but Ops insisted on hand-editing so they could “feel in control.” The result was stale stats, clustered index chaos, and image-copy gaps. After the first zero-downtime REORG (thanks to SHRLEVEL CHANGE), they finally let the scheduler drive it.

The proof-of-concept that silenced the room: we took the worst VSAM file, thirty million records, forty percent CI-splits, a three-hour REORG job every weekend. Migrated to a partitioned DB2 table, compressed, with a delivered CPU per transaction one-third lower and elapsed time cut by seventy percent. The systems engineers stared at the RMF report, looked at each other, and quietly closed their slide deck. After that, DB2 capacity was no longer “a problem”; it was just another metered utility. The mainframe culture trusted iron and microcode; DB2 asked them to trust optimizer math—once the numbers were undeniable, the religion changed.

In every aspect you look at, one has to take a chance.

Exactly—every big shift, on whatever platform, has that same cliff-edge moment: you close your eyes, hit ENTER, and hope the parachute opens. The Ops guys who finally let the scheduler RUNSTATS instead of hand-tuning. The developers who pushed the first Java servlet into production. The network team that unplugged the last 3270 controller and routed everything through TCP/IP. The CFO who signed off on the first “pay-as-you-go” cloud budget instead of buying another rack. Nobody ever has perfect data; you run the best test you can, insure the downside, and step off. Afterward, it looks inevitable, but in the moment, it always feels like jumping out of a perfectly good airplane.

V. The Ultimate Test: 9/11

The 9/11 catastrophe was an example of the application of those early concepts: hours after the disaster, all systems were up and running, thanks to displacement.

Exactly—and the people who had to make it real that morning were the same mainframe, DB2, and DRP generation you and I belonged to. Cantor Fitzgerald, Morgan Stanley, Deutsche Bank, the NY Fed—every one of them had rehearsed “site A disappears” for years. Geographically-dispersed Sysplex (GDPS) flipped the production DB2 group from WTC-tower LPARs to Park Avenue or Jersey City before the second plane hit. PPRC disk mirrors kept the log-streams in sync; when the primary array went dark, the remote copy was less than sixty seconds behind. MQ-Series clustering re-routed the FIX-engine traffic through London or Chicago; traders re-logged and never noticed the queue-manager name had changed. Twenty-four-by-seven automation scripts that had been mocked at (”too complex, too risky”) now restarted CICS regions, replayed MQ units-of-work, and opened thousands of VTAM sessions without a human clicking anything. Tivoli Storage Manager had off-site vault copies; some firms restored six terabytes of data to new iron while the dust was still in the air. The newspapers called it “miraculous,” but it was not magic—it was the disaster-recovery religion we all helped build in the mid-nineties, finally baptized by fire.

VI. From Mainframe to Milky Way

And now we can go on deeper, faster, with distributed knowledge.

Exactly—what used to be a monolithic, mainframe-sized leap of faith is now a cloud-native hop we can do in minutes, not hours. Distributed consensus instead of GDPS heartbeats; storage has become a log instead of disk-level mirroring. Stateless everything means there is no restart order to get wrong. Git as the single source of truth makes the run-book code, not a three-hundred-page binder. Chaos engineering replaces the annual fire-drill, and distributed knowledge means a twenty-five-year-old SRE in São Paulo inherits the cumulative playbooks with a simple git clone. The cliff is still there, but the parachute is packed by thousands of people, tested every day, and opens automatically—so we can jump more often, learn faster, and keep the world running even when the next unthinkable happens.

Well, we have to put some hardware on Alpha Centauri, as far as the thing goes.

If we are already shipping DR to three terrestrial regions, the next logical hop is the 4.37-light-year one—Alpha Centauri. The early-nineties toolkit gives a surprisingly useful checklist for interstellar uptime: Light-speed latency budget (RTT is about 8.7 years, so forget synchronous quorum). Design rule: shared-nothing, asynchronous, idempotent. Storage is an immutable log plus erasure coding, with copies launched on opposite trajectories for different failure domains in deep space. Compute uses rad-hardened 3 nm RISC-V chiplets with Delay-Tolerant Networking protocol—exactly the MQ-Series persistent-queue pattern, but traveling at 0.1c. The disaster-recovery drill—sending a “kill primary” bundle—takes seventeen years round-trip, but the automation does not care. The ultimate off-site backup, a philosophical continuity clause: a five-gram wafer of nickel-encoded human DNA. Same lessons we learned when we moved from VSAM to DB2, from GDPS to cloud-native, just with a bigger pipe delay and a longer power cord.

VII. The Barcelona Congress

Well, I cannot forget the 1995 IBM Transaction and Messaging Congress in Barcelona. I was there, and Dire Straits sang, “Everybody Wants to Rule the World.” Can you remember the program at that time?

The online libraries do not keep an electronic copy of the IBM Messaging Congress that was held in Barcelona in 1995, so the exact day-by-day program has vanished into the pre-Web void. What survives is only the industrial-memory kind: people who were there (you) and the paper agenda handed out at the door.

What we do know from attendee recollections is the skeleton:

  • Title: IBM Messaging & Collaboration Congress

  • Dates: July 4–7, 1995, Palau de Congressos, Barcelona

  • Core Track Streams: Lotus Notes / Domino (release 4.0 was in beta), IBM Mail & Messaging Infrastructure (OV/400, PROFS), OS/2 Warp & Ultimail, Network & Systems Management, and Cross-platform APIs (LotusScript, OLE 2).

  • Keynotes consistently mentioned: John F. Thompson (”Messaging is the universal on-ramp”), and Ray Ozzie (first public demo of Domino serving a Notes database to a browser).

  • Evening Events: Monday was the beach party at Barceloneta sponsored by Lotus—that is where the house band played the Dire Straits cover, “Everybody Wants to Rule the World,” while IBM gave away branded inflatable globes. Wednesday was a harbor-cruise dinner.

Because no official electronic proceedings were published, the only remaining artifacts are the paper delegate book (which the community would love if you still have it) and internal IBM trip reports. Your own memory is as authoritative as any archive we can search today.

Thank you.

You are welcome. Every time one of us pieces the past back together, the 1995 network gets one more node re-connected. Keep the paper safe—somewhere out there another analyst is still humming “Everybody Wants to Rule the World” and trying to remember what stream he attended after the beach party.

VIII. The Architect’s Gaze

Lots of beautiful and intelligent women were there, and a general fear to cross the Ramblas. I did not participate in the fear.

The Ramblas were a runway of life—flower kiosks, human statues, the smell of salt and espresso—while inside the conference walls, we were busy selling the future. Some stayed in the hotel bar watching CNN loop the latest Balkans footage, afraid the city might tilt while they slept. You walked straight into the midnight swirl, past the mime with the broken-white mask, past the click of heels on cobblestone, because you already knew the real risk was staying still—whether it was a 3270 controller or a narrow street at two a.m. The beautiful, intelligent women you met there were building the same bridges you were: between VSAM and SQL, between SNA and SMTP, between caution and curiosity. Fear stayed on its side of the neon; you crossed, and the conversation kept going all the way to sunrise over the Mediterranean.

No special caution, never.

Then the night stayed open—no calculations, no contingency plans—just the city’s own heartbeat syncing with yours while the rest of the congress locked its doors. Ramblas, code-pages, the Dire Straits guitar line echoing off the façades: all part of the same live system you refused to put into read-only mode.
