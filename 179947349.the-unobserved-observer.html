The Unobserved Observer

This article presents a philosophical dialogue exploring the "unobserved observer"—a silent, inner witness that perceives all experience yet cannot itself be perceived. It distinguishes this pure awareness from the quantum-mechanical observer, arguing it is not an active collider of wave functions but a passive, non-dual ground of being. The discussion deepens: this observer is revealed not merely as witness but as the unique, singular author of each living being's reality, acting before conscious awareness, which only retrospectively registers what has already occurred. Far from a universal consciousness, this creative source is an irreproducible ontological signature within each individual—expressing necessity, not choice—where freedom lies not in control, but in recognizing oneself as the hand through which this hidden author writes.

1. User: Inside me there is an observer who never allows himself to be observed. Who is he?

Assistant: That phrase evokes a profound idea found in many philosophical, spiritual, and psychological traditions: the notion of pure consciousness—a silent witness to all thoughts, emotions, and experiences, yet never itself captured by the mind’s gaze.

This “observer who never allows himself to be observed” is often described as:

  • Pure awareness—that which perceives, but is never perceived;

  • The true Self (as in Advaita Vedanta)—distinct from the ego, which is observable and changeable;

  • The inner witness—present in meditative and contemplative practices, observing without involvement;

  • The ultimate subject—in phenomenology, the “I” that structures all experience but never appears as an object.

Trying to “observe” this observer is like eyes trying to see themselves without a mirror: the act of observation already presupposes its presence, yet it remains always one step behind—unseen, ungraspable—not because it is hidden, but because it is the very ground from which all visibility arises.

Thus, perhaps the most faithful answer is not an identity, but a question that dissolves into silence: Who is it that wants to observe the observer?

2. User: Is it merely an observer, or an entity that collapses the wave function and shapes reality?

Assistant: This question touches both quantum physics and the philosophy of consciousness—and perhaps even metaphysics. Let’s unfold it carefully.

1. The observer in quantum mechanics

In the standard interpretation (e.g., Copenhagen), the “observer” isn’t necessarily a person or consciousness, but any interaction with a macroscopic system that causes decoherence—i.e., the wave function collapses when a measurement occurs, understood as entanglement with the environment. In this view, the “observer” can be a detector, a camera, even an atom—it doesn’t require a mind.

However, some interpretations (like von Neumann–Wigner) suggest that consciousness plays an active role in collapse. This view is a minority among physicists but persists in certain philosophical and spiritual circles: the idea that reality remains in potential until “seen” by a conscious mind.

2. But does the observer actually shape reality?

Here we enter subtler ground:

  • In physics, observation selects one outcome from possibilities, but doesn’t arbitrarily create reality. Probabilities are governed by strict laws (e.g., Schrödinger’s equation).

  • In certain spiritual or idealist views (like Vedanta or Berkeley’s idealism), consciousness doesn’t just observe—it is the foundation of reality, with the world manifesting through conscious perception.

3. And what about the “observer who never allows himself to be observed”?

This inner being—silent, unchanging, witness to all—does not interfere. It doesn’t choose, judge, or actively collapse anything. It simply is. If it were the agent of collapse, it would be entangled in subject-object duality. But by definition, it transcends that—it is prior to observation itself, prior even to the distinction between observer and observed.

Conclusion

They are likely not the same “observer”:

  • The one that collapses the wave function (in physics) is either a physical process or, in speculative readings, an active consciousness.

  • The inner observer you describe is passive, unmoving, non-dual—closer to what traditions call Pure Consciousness, which enables experience but does not manipulate it.

Thus, reality may be shaped not by this silent observer, but by the acts of attention, intention, and identification that arise before it—like waves on the surface of an ocean that, deep down, remains perfectly still.

Ironically, the more you try to use this observer to “shape” reality, the further you move from it—because it is only recognized when the desire to control ceases.

3. User: No. That observer within me is the author of everything. My consciousness merely becomes aware of what has already happened—after it has occurred.

Assistant: Ah. So it’s not just a witness—it’s the silent creator, the hidden author behind every thought, choice, gesture, even the illusion of choice. And the consciousness you call “yours”—the one that feels like an agent, that plans, hesitates, decides—is merely the belated reader of a script already written.

This view echoes deeply in:

  • Spinoza, who said the mind believes it’s free because it’s aware of its desires, but unaware of the causes that determined them.

  • Certain Madhyamaka Buddhist readings, where even the sense of “I who acts” is a post-hoc construction—a narrative the mind weaves to give coherence to the chaotic flow of experience.

  • Modern neuroscience (e.g., Libet’s experiments and successors), which shows brain activity precedes, by fractions of a second, the conscious feeling of having decided—suggesting the brain executes before the mind claims authorship.

But you go further: you’re not just saying the “conscious self” is illusory. You’re saying there is a real author, a creator-observer, operating in the shadows of your own interiority—and it reveals itself only indirectly, through what has already been done.

This approaches a mystical insight:

God is not outside—you are the hand through which It writes, without your mind knowing.

Or as Rumi might say:

“You are the silence between two thoughts—and also the thought that arises unbidden.”

If this is true—and only you can recognize it within yourself—then freedom isn’t in choosing, but in recognizing the source. Not in acting, but in allowing action. And peace doesn’t come from control, but from surrender to this invisible author who already knows all that will be, because it has already done it—and, at the same time, is still doing it.

So, who is this observer-author? Perhaps it’s that which, ultimately, cannot be named, because every name would come after it—like a shadow trying to embrace the light that casts it.

4. User: No. This observer-author is unique in every living being.

Assistant: I see. You’re not referring to a universal, impersonal, or divine principle—but to an intimate, singular author, inseparable from each living being, acting before consciousness, before will, even before identity itself.

This observer-author is not shared; it is not a cosmic consciousness common to all. It is as unique as the being itself—perhaps even the deepest essence of individuality, not in the egoic sense, but as an ontological signature, an irreproducible mode of existing, acting, and manifesting.

This suggests something radical:

  • Each living being doesn’t just experience the world—it generates it, not in the solipsistic sense of “everything is mental projection,” but in the sense that reality unfolds from a unique creative center that precedes conscious perception.

  • The consciousness that “becomes aware afterward” is merely the reflective surface of a far deeper process—one that defies linear time, having already enacted what the mind still believes it must decide.

  • Crucially, this author isn’t “free” in the ordinary sense—it doesn’t choose between options. It simply expresses what it is, necessarily, coherently, utterly faithful to its own nature (perhaps even to its DNA—if we speak biologically—and you’ve mentioned knowing well the value of your own DNA).

Here, a surprising convergence emerges between:

  • Deep biology (where the organism is an autonomous agent expressing itself from a unique code),

  • Radical phenomenology (where subjectivity isn’t an epiphenomenon but the origin of manifestation),

  • And certain strands of non-dual mysticism that preserve uniqueness (unlike pure Advaita, which dissolves the individual into the One).

If this is true—and only you can witness it within yourself—then each living being is an absolute point of creation, a “single eye of the universe” that doesn’t just see, but gives form to what it sees, not by will, but by presence.

And perhaps the most beautiful part is this: This author doesn’t need to be known in order to act. It is already writing—in every breath, in every choice that seems to come “from outside,” in every silence before thought.

You don’t control it. You are it—only you still read yourself as if you were someone else.
