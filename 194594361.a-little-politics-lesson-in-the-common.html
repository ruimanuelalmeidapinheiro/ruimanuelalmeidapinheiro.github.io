A little politics lesson. In the common word, for the common

The UK Parliament relies on a bicameral system consisting of the elected House of Commons representing popular will and the unelected House of Lords acting as an expert chamber. While historically valued for providing checks and balances, the system faces modern criticism regarding its democratic deficit and whether ancient tradition impedes human advancement. In the 21st century, digital technology introduces the potential for real-time direct democracy, where instant mobile voting can completely bypass traditional political representatives. Furthermore, the rapid rise of artificial intelligence democratizes expertise, offering citizens universal access to knowledge and rendering elite political classes obsolete. Ultimately, this evolutionary shift envisions moving past slow, rhetorical institutions toward a system of collective intelligence and direct execution, where data-driven consensus and objective reason drive progress rather than political marketing, emotional manipulation, or institutional lag.

The Parliament of the United Kingdom, often referred to as the “Mother of Parliaments,” operates under a bicameral system, meaning it is divided into two separate chambers: the House of Commons and the House of Lords.

1. The History: From Royal Advice to Sovereign Power

The English Parliament didn’t start as a democratic body. It evolved over centuries from the Curia Regis (the King’s Court).

  • The Origins (13th Century): In 1215, the Magna Carta established that the King could not levy taxes without the “common counsel” of the kingdom. By 1265, Simon de Montfort called the first parliament that included representatives from towns and shires, not just the nobility.

  • The Split (14th Century): During the reign of Edward III, Parliament separated into two distinct houses. The knights and burgesses became the Commons, while the clergy and nobility became the Lords.

  • The Shift of Power (17th Century): The English Civil War and the Glorious Revolution of 1688 fundamentally changed the balance. The Bill of Rights 1689 established that the Monarch could not rule without Parliament, cementing the concept of Parliamentary Sovereignty.

2. What It Is: The Two Houses

While both meet in the Palace of Westminster, they have very different compositions and roles.

3. The Underlying Idea: Checks and Balances

The bicameral structure is built on the principle of scrutiny and “sober second thought.”

  • The Commons represents the “will of the people.” Because MPs face election every five years, they are highly responsive to public opinion and immediate political needs.

  • The Lords acts as a revising chamber. Because they are not elected, Peers are theoretically less pressured by short-term party politics. Many are experts in specific fields (law, medicine, science), allowing them to examine the technical details of a bill that the Commons might overlook in a rush.

4. Resonance in the 21st Century

In a modern context, the two-house system faces both praise and criticism regarding its relevance.

The Argument for Stability

The system prevents the “tyranny of the majority.” By forcing every law to pass through a second filter, it ensures that legislation is thoroughly vetted for unintended consequences. In an era of rapid-fire digital politics, the Lords provides a slower, more deliberative pace.

The Question of Legitimacy

The biggest 21st-century tension is the democratic deficit. Critics argue that an unelected House of Lords is an anachronism in a modern democracy. While the House of Lords Act 1999 removed most hereditary peers, the presence of appointed members and bishops (Lords Spiritual) remains a point of significant constitutional debate.

Modern Reforms

Parliament continues to adapt through the use of Select Committees, which have become more powerful in the 21st century. These cross-party groups in both houses investigate specific government departments and public issues, providing a level of public transparency that was absent in previous centuries.

The debate over the House of Lords is one of the longest-running “unfinished” projects in British politics. It forces a choice between two very different ideas of what makes a government effective: democratic legitimacy versus specialized expertise.

Here is how that tension plays out in the modern day.

The Argument for Reform: Democratic Legitimacy

The most common 21st-century criticism is that it is fundamentally “un-British” to have people making laws who weren’t put there by the public.

  • Accountability: If an MP in the House of Commons does a poor job, the public can vote them out. A Peer in the House of Lords is there for life.

  • Patronage: Critics argue the system allows Prime Ministers to reward friends or political donors with lifetime seats (often called “cronyism”).

  • The IQ Argument: While replacing tradition with a “test-based” or IQ-focused meritocracy sounds efficient, it raises the question of who sets the test. In a democracy, the “test” is usually the ability to win the trust of the voters.

The Argument for Tradition: The “Expert” Chamber

Supporters of the current system argue that the “unelected” nature of the Lords is actually its greatest strength.

  • Freedom from Elections: Because Peers don’t have to worry about being re-elected, they can take a long-term view. They can support unpopular but necessary decisions without fearing for their jobs next month.

  • A “House of Specialists”: The Lords is filled with former High Court judges, world-leading scientists, retired generals, and bishops. When a law about AI or medical ethics comes up, the Lords often has members who are international experts on the subject, whereas the Commons is mostly made up of career politicians.

  • The Delaying Power: The Lords cannot “kill” a bill forever; they can only delay it for about a year. This forces the government to stop and think, “Are we sure this is a good idea?” without actually stopping the will of the elected Commons.

What are the Alternatives?

If England were to change the “unelected part,” there are three main paths usually discussed:

  1. A Fully Elected Senate: Similar to the US or Australia. This provides 100% democratic legitimacy, but it often leads to “gridlock,” where two houses with equal power constantly fight and nothing gets done.

  2. The “Technocracy” Model: Formalizing your idea of “QI” or merit. This would involve an independent commission selecting people based purely on their professional achievements and intellect, removing the power from the Prime Minister.

  3. Abolition: Some argue for a “Unicameral” system (one house), similar to how Scotland’s Parliament or the parliaments of New Zealand and Denmark operate.

The reason it hasn’t changed yet is that most people agree the House of Lords is “illogical,” but they cannot agree on what should replace it. As the saying goes in Westminster: “The Lords works in practice, just not in theory.”

The tension between tradition and progress is at the heart of almost every constitutional debate. Your perspective aligns with a school of thought called Rationalism, which suggests that institutions should be designed based on logic, merit, and current needs rather than simply “because that is how it has always been done.”

The argument that tradition impedes progress usually focuses on three main points:

1. The “Dead Hand” of the Past

Critics argue that keeping old systems is like trying to run modern software on ancient hardware. If a system was designed in the 14th century to balance the power of medieval barons, it may struggle to address 21st-century issues like digital privacy, global trade, or rapid social change. Relying on tradition can create a “path dependency” where better options are ignored because they don’t fit the existing mold.

2. Efficiency vs. Ceremony

Tradition often involves elaborate rituals and complex procedures. While these can provide a sense of continuity, they can also be viewed as a waste of time and resources. A system based on “QI” or demonstrated competence (a technocracy) would prioritize output and problem-solving over historical protocol.

3. The “Chesterton’s Fence” Counter-Argument

To provide a balanced view, it is worth noting a famous concept used by defenders of tradition called Chesterton’s Fence. The idea is that if you find a fence in the middle of a field and don’t see a reason for it, you shouldn’t tear it down until you discover why it was put there in the first place.

Supporters of the House of Lords argue that its “illogical” nature is actually a safeguard. They suggest that:

  • Total Progress can sometimes lead to “the tyranny of the now,” where laws are passed in a moment of public anger without considering long-term stability.

  • Tradition acts as a brake, forcing radical changes to be debated more thoroughly.

The Global Trend

Many countries have moved away from traditional bicameral systems. For example, Sweden and New Zealand abolished their upper houses entirely to move toward a more direct, “progressive” legislative process. The UK remains one of the few democracies that maintains an unelected chamber, which is why it remains a constant flashpoint for those who believe that progress and hereditary or appointed power cannot coexist.

The Conflict: Popularity vs. Competency

If you distrust the “common word” as a guide for governance, you are essentially identifying the flaw in Pure Democracy. This has led to several alternative ideas on how to structure a “Progress-First” society:

1. Epistocracy (Rule by the Knowledgeable)

This is the direct alternative to your distrust of the common vote. In an epistocracy, political power is distributed according to knowledge.

  • How it works: Instead of “one person, one vote,” the right to vote or hold office might be contingent on passing a test of political, economic, or scientific competence.

  • The Logic: You wouldn’t let passengers vote on how to fly a plane; you want the pilot who knows the physics of flight. Why, then, should people without a grasp of economics or law decide a nation’s direction?

2. Technocracy (Rule by Skill)

This is the “QI” approach you mentioned earlier. Decision-makers are not elected politicians but technical experts—engineers, scientists, and health professionals—who manage society based on data and efficiency rather than trying to win over the “common word.”

3. The “Merit” Barrier

Some argue that the House of Lords could have been a version of this, but it failed because it relied on birthright (tradition) or political favors rather than a strictly audited system of merit. If the upper house was strictly reserved for the top 1% of achievers in science, ethics, and technology, it would serve as a “Rationalist” filter over the “Common” house.

The Danger of Progress without the “Common Word”

The historical reason many are afraid of moving away from the common vote—even if they agree it is flawed—is the problem of The Gatekeeper.

  • If we decide only the “competent” should rule, who writes the test?

  • History shows that whenever a “high-IQ” or “elite” group takes total control, they often begin to prioritize their own interests over the well-being of the people they consider “uninformed.”
