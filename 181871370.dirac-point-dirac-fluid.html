Dirac Point, Dirac Fluid

The Dirac Point is a unique feature in the electronic band structure of materials like graphene where conduction and valence bands touch, creating massless quasiparticles with linear energy-momentum dispersion. When tuned near this point under specific conditions—strong electron-electron interactions, low temperatures, and high sample purity—electrons and holes form a collective hydrodynamic state known as the Dirac Fluid. In this regime, charge carriers behave like a viscous quantum fluid rather than independent particles. The key distinction: the Dirac Point is a single-particle band structure property, while the Dirac Fluid is a many-body phenomenon emerging from strong interactions on that foundation, offering insights into relativistic quantum hydrodynamics in condensed matter systems.

1. Dirac Point

What it is:
The Dirac Point is a unique location in the electronic band structure of certain materials where the conduction and valence bands touch at a single point (or a set of points) in momentum space. Unlike traditional semiconductors or insulators, there is no energy gap at this point.

Key Characteristics:

  • Linear Dispersion: Near the Dirac point, the energy-momentum relationship is linear (E ∝ k), not parabolic as in most materials. This mimics the behavior of relativistic massless particles described by the Dirac equation (hence the name).

  • Massless Quasiparticles: The electrons (or holes) behave as if they are massless Dirac fermions, moving at a constant Fermi velocity (~1/300 the speed of light in graphene).

  • Zero Density of States: At the exact Dirac point, the density of electronic states goes to zero.

  • Neutrality Point: When the Fermi energy is tuned to the Dirac point (e.g., by electrostatic gating), the material switches from electron conduction to hole conduction. The carrier concentration is minimal, leading to a local maximum in resistivity.

Where it’s found:

  • Graphene: The most famous example. Its honeycomb lattice produces two inequivalent Dirac points (K and K’) in its Brillouin zone.

  • Topological Insulators: Surface states host Dirac cones.

  • Some 3D Materials: Such as Dirac semimetals (e.g., Cd₃As₂, Na₃Bi).

Significance:
The Dirac point is responsible for many of graphene’s extraordinary electronic properties, such as ultra-high mobility, the half-integer quantum Hall effect, and Klein tunneling.

2. Dirac Fluid

What it is:
The Dirac Fluid is a collective, hydrodynamic regime of the electron-hole plasma in a system with Dirac points (like graphene) under specific conditions. It refers to a state where electrons and holes strongly interact with each other, ceasing to behave as individual particles and instead acting like a viscous, fluid-like quantum soup.

Key Conditions for its Formation:

  1. Near the Dirac Point: The Fermi energy must be tuned close to the Dirac point, creating a near-equal density of electrons and holes.

  2. Strong Electron-Electron Interactions: The rate of electron-electron collisions (τ_ee⁻¹) must become the fastest scattering process, dominating over:

    • Electron-impurity scattering (τ_imp⁻¹)

    • Electron-phonon scattering (τ_ph⁻¹)

  3. Clean Samples & Low Temperatures: To minimize impurity/defect scattering and phonon scattering, allowing the intrinsic interactions to prevail.

Hallmarks of Dirac Fluid Behavior:

  • Viscous Electron Flow: Unlike ballistic or diffusive flow, the fluid exhibits vortices, Poiseuille flow profiles, and negative nonlocal resistance.

  • Enhanced Coulomb Drag: Strong momentum transfer between closely spaced layers.

  • Anomalous Thermal Properties: The Wiedemann-Franz law (relating electrical and thermal conductivity) is violated. The fluid carries heat very efficiently relative to charge.

  • Collective Excitations: The plasma oscillates in a way that is characteristic of a fluid, not a gas of independent particles.

Analogy:
Think of ordinary electrons in metals as a rarefied gas (they collide with walls/impurities more than with each other). The Dirac fluid is like water or honey—particles interact constantly with each other, leading to collective viscous behavior.

Relationship Between the Two

  • The Dirac Point provides the stage—it creates the massless, symmetric spectrum of electrons and holes.

  • The Dirac Fluid is a specific collective phase that emerges on that stage when conditions (temperature, cleanliness, doping) are just right to make interactions dominate.

In short: The Dirac point is a single-particle band structure feature. The Dirac fluid is a many-body hydrodynamic phenomenon that can occur in systems possessing Dirac points.

Experimental Proof: Landmark experiments in 2016-2019, particularly on ultraclean graphene at temperatures around 50-100K, measured hydrodynamic flow patterns and violation of the Wiedemann-Franz law, providing strong evidence for the Dirac fluid’s existence.
