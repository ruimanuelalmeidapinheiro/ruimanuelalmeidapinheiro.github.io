📖 Life & career journey

1953 · Birth
Born in Arroios, Lisbon. Early years in a city full of light and transformation.
1969 · Medical school fascination
At 16, entered the Faculty of Medicine in Lisbon. After six months, left fascinated by DNA & RNA discoveries — the early sparks of structural logic.
1970s · Stock exchange intensity
Worked at a brokerage firm on the Lisbon stock exchange. "Wow, what a life! The work was intense, but the money came in fast and easy."
1974 · The Carnation Revolution
April 25, 1974 — unemployed after communists shut down the Lisbon stock exchange. A turning point.
1975 · Start at CGD
Joined CGD (Caixa Geral de Depósitos) from the bottom, level 3 or 4. Worked on State accounts before discovering the IT revolution.
1978 · Organization & IT department
We were 12 candidates and we entered. The gateway to Business Science and IBM mainframes — a lifelong passion.
1984 · DB2 Mainframe development
Dedicated exclusively to application development for Db2 Mainframe databases. Mastered machine language, Assembler, COBOL, ISPF, PROLOG.
1990s · VSAM to DB2 migration
Led the structural migration wave. Tackled the Year 2000 challenge with date representation in Data Sets.
1999 · Illness & retirement
After a change in directors and restructuring, became seriously ill and retired. But curiosity never retired.
2010–2020 · Internet & PC resurgence
Kept pace with information technologies, building homelab experiments and revisiting systems thinking.
Aug 2022 · Prompt engineering
Took several courses in prompt engineering — became fluent with LLMs. "Indispensable tools for development and knowledge acquisition."
Jan 2025 · Content Engineering
Defining the strategic blueprint for structured content. Purpose, audience, core messages, modular components, governance workflows, and scalable content models.
Oct 2025 · AI workflow management
Applying artificial intelligence to automate, optimize, and govern business processes. Real-time adaptation, predictive analytics, self-improving systems.
2026 · Foundational physics, epistemic pipelines, book and social media

In 2026 I proceed my formation in physics, biology, and computer science, dedicating about fourteen hours per day.

This is not a return to school but a deepening of the work I began with AI workflow management and content engineering.

I am developing a personal theoretical framework that questions where mainstream physics assumes rather than proves. For that framework I have designed a set of stress tests, detailed in a Zenodo paper. I have designed six stress tests: derivation, asymmetry, quantization, binding, effectiveness.

The paper is lean; the tests are cruel. Any framework that bends to them survives.

Alongside the framework, I am building an epistemic‑first arXiv ingestion pipeline with mainstream accepted physics domain taxonomy.

The pipeline ingests preprints nightly, classifying them by how they ground claims: empirical, mathematical, analogical, or mystical.

It outputs a map of what physics actually trusts — and where it hides its ambiguities.

I am writing a book about mainstream assumptions, perceptual limits, and wave function collapse.

The book has lots of equations, all that is maiinstream accepted.

It walks through each assumption — objectivity, locality, separability — and then maps the limits of human perception.

The final third treats collapse not as a problem to solve but as a residue of description.

I am also developing a custom LLM from first principles, one that aims to be useful.

Useful means it reads my arXiv pipeline output, highlights structural gaps in my framework, and helps rewrite the book’s arguments against their own weaknesses.

It is built token by token, not fine‑tuned from anyone else's weights.

I maintain an active written presence across Substack, Medium, and other social media and European Countries sites.
