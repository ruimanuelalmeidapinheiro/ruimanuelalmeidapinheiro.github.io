“Who references the existence of Christ?”

The Letters of Paul of Tarsus (or Pauline Epistles) are a set of 13 books of the New Testament of the Bible. Written around 37 AD to 60 AD, they are considered the oldest Christian texts in existence and fundamental to the theology of the Church.

Below, an overview of these letters and their context:

✍️ What are they and why were they written?

Unlike the gospels, which tell the story of Jesus, these are pastoral and theological letters. Paul wrote them to guide, resolve conflicts, and explain doctrine to the Christian communities he founded throughout the Mediterranean.

Authorship and Dating: The majority were written by the apostle himself. Scholars point out that Romans, Corinthians, and Galatians are indisputably authentic, dating from the mid-1st century. The “Pastoral Letters” (1 and 2 Timothy, Titus) may have been written by later disciples, consolidating his legacy.

📜 The 13 Letters of the New Testament

In the Bible, they are generally ordered by length (from longest to shortest):

  • Romans: The most systematic. Presents the doctrine of salvation by faith to a community he did not found.

  • 1 and 2 Corinthians: Address internal problems, immorality, and doctrines such as resurrection and spiritual gifts.

  • Galatians: Defends Christian freedom against the imposition of Jewish laws (circumcision). It was crucial for the Protestant Reformation.

  • Ephesians, Philippians, and Colossians: Known as the “Prison Letters,” written in custody. Focus on the nature of the Church, the humility of Christ, and his supremacy.

  • 1 and 2 Thessalonians: Deal with the second coming of Christ (Eschatology).

  • 1 and 2 Timothy and Titus (Pastoral): Practical advice on leadership, organization, and combating false doctrines.

  • Philemon: A short, personal letter interceding for a runaway slave named Onesimus.

🌍 Historical Context: “Three Worlds”

To understand the letters, one must visualize Paul in three cultural scenarios:

  • Roman: He used his Roman citizenship and the infrastructure of the Empire to travel. The Roman concept of Pax (Peace) was contrasted with the “Peace of Christ.”

  • Greek: He wrote in Greek (Koine). He used terms from Greek philosophy and rhetorical techniques to dialogue with intellectuals.

  • Jewish: A Pharisee, “a Hebrew of Hebrews.” His argumentation is full of references to the Old Testament and Jewish reasoning.

“The gospels that reference Christ?”

When we ask about the gospels that reference Christ, it is important to clarify: all canonical gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) have Jesus Christ as their central theme. The difference lies in how they present Him.

If you refer to gospels that explicitly call Jesus “Christ” (Messiah/Anointed), the four canonical ones do so. But if the question aims at gospels that emphasize the divinity and the title “Christ” theologically, we can highlight:

📖 Canonical Gospels (in the Bible)

  • Matthew: Presents Jesus as the Christ, Son of David (Messiah promised to Israel). Uses “Christ” from the first verse (Mt 1:1).

  • Mark: Begins by calling Him “Jesus Christ, Son of God” (Mk 1:1). Focus on messianic action.

  • Luke: Shows Jesus as the Christ anointed by the Spirit (Lk 4:18), universal Savior.

  • John: Reveals Jesus as the Christ, the Son of God (Jn 20:31), with maximum emphasis on divinity (the incarnate Word).

📜 Apocryphal Gospels (not in the Bible)

Some also reference Christ, but with different views:

  • Gospel of Peter: Jesus is portrayed as divine, but with docetic traits (he suffered without really feeling pain).

  • Gospel of Thomas: Contains sayings of Jesus, but does not develop the title “Christ” as in the canonical ones.

  • Gospel of Philip: Speaks of Jesus Christ as a Gnostic figure, linked to mysteries.

✨ Conclusion

If you seek the gospels that most explicitly and theologically develop Jesus as the divine Christ, the four canonical ones are the answer, especially John and Matthew. The apocryphal ones reference him, but with later doctrines not accepted by Christian orthodoxy.

“Roman sources that cite Christ”

Yes, there are important Roman sources that cite Christ. Although no 1st-century Roman historian wrote about Jesus during his lifetime, some authors from the early 2nd century provide valuable and independent references. The main ones are:

🏛️ Tacitus (Annals, c. 116 AD)

The most important reference comes from the Roman historian Cornelius Tacitus. In recounting the great fire of Rome in 64 AD and the subsequent persecution of Nero against Christians, he explains the origin of the name “Christians”:

“Christ, from whom the name derives, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius, at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilate.” (Annales 15.44)

Why it is reliable:

  • Tacitus was a Roman senator and a respected historian, with access to official archives (Acta Senatus).

  • He felt contempt for Christians (calling their belief a “pernicious superstition”), which makes it extremely unlikely that he invented or repeated a favorable Christian story.

  • His mention independently confirms three central facts: the existence of Christ, his execution under Pontius Pilate, and the context of the reign of Tiberius.

📜 Pliny the Younger (Letters, c. 111–113 AD)

As governor of the province of Bithynia, Pliny wrote to Emperor Trajan asking for instructions on how to deal with Christians. In his letter, he describes their practices:

“They affirmed [...] that their only fault [...] was that they had the custom of gathering before dawn on a certain fixed day, when they would responsively sing verses of a hymn to Christ, treating him as God.” (Letters 10.96)

This letter is important because it shows that, less than a century after Jesus’s death, Christians already worshipped him as God — a very rapid historical development for a “later myth.”

😂 Lucian of Samosata (The Death of Peregrine, c. 165 AD)

A Greek satirist who mocked Christians, involuntarily confirming details about their faith:

“And they regarded him as their protector and had him as their lawgiver [...] the man who was crucified in Palestine for giving rise to this cult.”

His mockery that Christians worshipped “the crucified sophist” is external testimony that Jesus was a historical figure executed in Palestine.

⚠️ Suetonius (The Life of the Twelve Caesars, c. 121 AD)

Mentions agitation among the Jews in Rome “impelled by Chrestus” during the reign of Claudius (c. 49–50 AD). Most scholars believe that “Chrestus” is a variation of “Christus” (Christ), indirectly referring to Jesus as the cause of the division between Jews and Christians.

💎 Conclusion

Although Roman records about Jesus’s life are scarce — something expected for an itinerant preacher from a remote province — the mentions by Tacitus and Pliny are particularly valuable. They confirm, in an independent and even hostile manner, the existence of Jesus, his execution under Pilate, and the cult dedicated to him as God in the early second century.

“I want you to identify more unreferenced sources.”

Yes, besides the Roman sources already mentioned (Tacitus, Pliny, and Suetonius), there are other ancient references to Christ in non-Christian documents, including a Syrian philosopher, the Jewish historian Flavius Josephus, and rabbinic texts. These are particularly valuable for being independent or even hostile to primitive Christianity.

Here are the main ones:

📜 Mara bar Sarapion (c. 73 AD)

A Syrian Stoic philosopher who wrote a letter to his son, comparing the fate of unjustly treated wise men. He refers to Jesus as the “wise king” of the Jews:

“What advantage did the Jews gain by killing their wise king?... Yet their kingdom was not taken from them because of it.”

Although he does not name Jesus directly, most scholars identify this figure as him. The letter independently confirms that Jesus was seen as a wise and just leader, executed by the Jews, and that his teachings survived him.

✡️ Flavius Josephus (c. 94 AD)

The most important Jewish historian of the 1st century. In his Jewish Antiquities, Josephus mentions Jesus twice. The most famous passage, the Testimonium Flavianum, is controversial because scholars believe Christian copyists modified it.

However, a 10th-century Arabic version, cited by Bishop Agapius, preserves what is probably the original text, without the Christian additions. This version says:

“At that time, a wise man named Jesus... Pilate condemned him to be crucified and killed. But his disciples... reported that he appeared to them on the third day... and that for this reason he might be the Messiah.”

The second mention, generally accepted as authentic, refers to the stoning of James, “the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ.”

🕍 Rabbinic Sources (Talmud)

Later Jewish writings (2nd–5th centuries AD) confirm the existence of Jesus, although in a negative and polemical form. The Jewish researcher Joseph Klausner summarizes these references:

  • Name and origin: Yeshua (Yeshu) of Nazareth.

  • Activities: “Practiced sorcery” (reference to miracles), “seduced and led Israel astray” (seen as a false prophet).

  • Death: Was “hanged on a tree” (crucified) as a false teacher on the eve of Passover.

“And about Mary Magdalene?”

Here are the main points about her:

📖 Evidence in the Gospels

In the four canonical gospels, Mary Magdalene is presented as a figure of prominence and extreme loyalty:

  • Healing and Discipleship: Luke mentions that Jesus cast out seven demons from her, and she became one of the women who followed and served him with her means, witnessing his ministry in Galilee.

  • Witness of the Crucifixion: She was at the foot of the cross when many disciples fled, showing immense courage (Mark 15:40; Matthew 27:56; John 19:25).

  • First Witness of the Resurrection: This is her most crucial role. She went to the tomb on Sunday morning, found it empty, and was the first person to whom the risen Jesus appeared, being sent to announce the resurrection to the other disciples (John 20:11–18).

⚔️ The Confusion with the “Sinner” and Mary of Bethany

Throughout history, a tradition arose that merged Mary Magdalene with two other women:

  1. The Anonymous “Sinner”: Who anointed Jesus’s feet with tears and perfume (Luke 7:36–50).

  2. Mary of Bethany: Sister of Martha and Lazarus, who also anointed Jesus (John 12:1–8).

Western tradition (the Roman Catholic Church) long considered these three to be the same person. Eastern tradition (the Greek Church) and most modern Protestant critics distinguish them, pointing out that the gospels never make this direct identification. The confusion, in part, consolidated the popular image of Mary Magdalene as a “repentant prostitute,” a label the original texts never assign her.

📜 Origins and Unreferenced Sources

The name “Magdalene” probably indicates her city of origin: Magdala, a village on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee, near Tiberias.

There is a fascinating controversy involving rabbinic sources:

  • The “Hairdresser”: The Talmud mentions a “Miriam megaddela se’ar” (Mary, the braider/arranger of women’s hair).

  • Ambiguous Identification: Some medieval scholars (Tosafot) identified this woman as the mother of Jesus, while modern scholars suggest it may be a polemical distortion of Mary Magdalene. In this view, “Magdalene” would be a pun on the Aramaic term for “hairdresser,” used in Jewish texts to make defamatory accusations about her morality.

✨ Mary Magdalene in Other Sources

Besides the gospels, Mary Magdalene appears in apocryphal texts, which did not become part of the biblical canon:

  • Gospel of Philip (3rd c. AD): Describes her as the “companion” (or koinōnos, a term that can mean spiritual partner) of Jesus, whom he loved more than the other disciples. It is the source of the famous image of Jesus kissing Magdalene.

  • Gospel of Mary Magdalene (2nd c. AD): Presents her as a visionary leader who received special revelations from Jesus, generating conflict with Peter over the leadership of the movement.

“Who was the pope who in the 11th century rewrote the New Testament?”

In fact, there was no pope who rewrote the Bible in the 11th century. The biblical canon (the list of books that make up the Bible) had already been widely established by the Church since the early centuries of Christianity.

However, you are probably referring to one of the two important historical events of that period involving papal authority or the organization of the Church:

1. The Gregorian Reform (Pope Gregory VII)

Pope Gregory VII (1073–1085) was the most central figure of the 11th century. He did not rewrite the biblical text, but he “rewrote” the rules of the Church.

  • Dictatus Papae: He published a decree asserting the absolute authority of the Pope over the Church and over worldly empires.

  • Liturgical Unification: He worked to standardize the rites and texts used in masses, eliminating local variations in favor of the Roman rite.

2. The Great Schism (1054)

In this century, the definitive separation occurred between the Roman Catholic Church and the Orthodox Church. Although it did not change the content of the Bible, this event permanently altered how Scripture was interpreted and which traditions would be followed by each side.

Why does this myth exist?

Often, the idea that the Bible was “rewritten” arises from confusion with:

  • Translations: In the 11th century, the Bible used was the Latin Vulgate (translated by Saint Jerome in the 4th century). There was no official new translation that altered the meaning of the text at that time.

  • Later translation councils: There were textual revisions and corrections of copying errors made by monks (scribes), but nothing that changed the doctrine or the books contained in it.

If you are thinking of a specific conspiracy theory or a ruler who tried to influence religion, it may be confusion with Emperor Constantine (4th century) or King James (17th century), who are frequently (though often incorrectly) associated with the idea of “changing” the Bible.

“I am thinking of concrete facts about the writing of the version of the New Testament read in Catholic churches.”

To understand the version of the New Testament read in Catholic churches today, one must look at a process of centuries involving translation, textual criticism, and standardization. There was no arbitrary “rewriting,” but rather a continuous effort to maintain fidelity to the original manuscripts.

Here are the concrete facts about how this text was formed and established:

1. The Latin Vulgate (The Initial Landmark)

Until the 4th century, there were several fragmented Latin translations (known as Vetus Latina). Pope Damasus I commissioned Saint Jerome to produce a unified and accurate translation.

  • The Process: Jerome translated the New Testament from the oldest and most reliable Greek manuscripts available at the time (4th century).

  • Result: This version became the Vulgate, which was the standard Bible of the Catholic Church for more than 1,500 years.

2. The Council of Trent (16th Century)

The question of the “official version” became critical during the Protestant Reformation. In 1546, the Council of Trent declared the Vulgate as the authentic edition for public use, preaching, and exposition.

  • Important Note: The objective was not to change the text, but to ensure that everyone used the same translation to avoid doctrinal errors in a period of great theological conflict.

3. The New Vulgate (The Contemporary Version)

The version that serves as the basis for the readings you hear at Mass today (the Lectionaries) is the New Vulgate.

  • Promulgation: It was declared the “typical” edition (official reference) by Pope John Paul II in 1979.

  • What changed: Experts revised Jerome’s Latin by comparing it with advances in biblical archaeology and linguistics of the 20th century. The goal was to bring the text even closer to the Greek originals (for the New Testament) and Hebrew (for the Old).

4. From the Original Languages to Portuguese

Although the New Vulgate is the official Vatican reference, modern Catholic Bibles and Mass readings in Portugal or Brazil are not “second-hand” translations (from Latin to Portuguese).

  • Direct Translation: Episcopal conferences use translations made directly from the Koine Greek (the original language of the New Testament) into Portuguese.

  • Liturgical Language: The text is adjusted to be proclaimed aloud, ensuring clarity and reverence, while maintaining formal equivalence with the original Greek.

5. Textual Criticism and Manuscripts

The current Catholic version is based on the so-called Critical Apparatus. Instead of using only one manuscript, translators compare thousands of ancient fragments (such as the Codex Vaticanus and the Codex Sinaiticus) to determine which was, with the highest probability, the exact phrase written by the apostles.

Summary of the Text’s Lineage

  1. Originals (1st Century): Written in Koine Greek.

  2. Vulgate (4th Century): Jerome’s translation into Latin.

  3. New Vulgate (1979): Scientific revision of the Latin text.

  4. National Translations (Current): Direct translation from Greek into Portuguese, authorized by the local Church.

“Which pope and when changed the calendar?”

The pope who changed the calendar was Pope Gregory XIII. The change occurred in the year 1582, through the papal bull Inter gravissimas.

Here are the concrete facts about this transition:

1. The Reason for the Change

The previous calendar, the Julian Calendar (instituted by Julius Caesar in 46 BC), had a calculation error regarding the duration of the solar year. It assumed that the year had exactly 365.25 days, when in fact it is about 11 minutes shorter.

  • Over the centuries, this error accumulated. By the 16th century, the calendar was 10 days misaligned with respect to the seasons of the year and the equinoxes.

  • For the Church, the main problem was calculating the date of Easter, which was progressively moving away from spring.

2. The Solution: The Leap in Time

To correct the accumulated error and prevent it from repeating, Gregory XIII implemented two drastic measures in October 1582:

  • Elimination of 10 days: The day after October 4, 1582 was, by decree, October 15, 1582. People went to bed on a Thursday and woke up on a Friday, ten days later.

  • New rule for leap years: To maintain precision in the future, it was defined that centennial years (ending in 00) would only be leap years if divisible by 400 (e.g.: 1600 and 2000 were leap years, but 1700, 1800, and 1900 were not).

3. The Implementation

The change was not immediately accepted by everyone:

  • Catholic Countries: Portugal, Spain, Italy, and Poland adopted the new calendar immediately, in 1582.

  • Protestant and Orthodox Countries: They resisted for decades or centuries, seeing the change as an imposition of papal authority. Great Britain only adopted it in 1752 and Russia only in 1918.

This system, known as the Gregorian Calendar, is what we officially use in most of the world today.

“The celebration of Spring (not Stravinsky’s) coincides with the beginning of the year in Neolithic cultures.”

You are entirely right to point out this connection. The association of the beginning of the year with the Spring Equinox (in the northern hemisphere) is one of the oldest milestones of humanity, based on direct observation of biological and astronomical cycles.

Here are the points that link this celebration to Neolithic roots and how it collided with later reforms:

1. The Awakening of the Earth as Ground Zero

In Neolithic societies, survival depended on agriculture. The beginning of the year was not an arbitrary date, but the moment when the soil “was reborn.”

  • Symbolism: The equinox represents balance (equal days and nights) and the return of light.

  • Archaeological Evidence: Monuments such as Stonehenge or the megaliths of Almendres in Portugal are aligned to capture the sun at the solstices and equinoxes, serving as stone calendars to mark these fundamental transitions.

2. The Legacy in Later Calendars

This Neolithic heritage persisted in civilizations that inherited this knowledge:

  • Original Roman Calendar: Before Julius Caesar, the Roman year began in March (the month of Mars). That is why, even today, the months of September, October, November, and December have names derived from 7, 8, 9, and 10, respectively, although they are now the 9th to 12th months.

  • Nowruz: The Persian New Year, celebrated for more than 3,000 years, coincides precisely with the March equinox and maintains the tradition of the renewal of nature.

3. The Tension with the Gregorian Calendar

When Pope Gregory XIII reformed the calendar in 1582 (as we mentioned before), the focus was technical and liturgical, not agricultural.

  • The Church needed the equinox fixed on March 21 to be precise in order to calculate Easter.

  • By fixing the beginning of the civil year on January 1 (a Roman heritage that had been unstable during the Middle Ages), the administrative structure of the Church and the State moved away from the “rhythm of the earth” that Neolithic cultures rigorously followed.

4. Easter as a “Survivor”

Although the civil year was moved to winter, the Catholic Easter remains the great vestige of that Neolithic lunisolar calendar within Christianity. It is celebrated on the first Sunday after the first full moon that occurs following the spring equinox. It is, in its essence, a celebration of life that sprouts after the death of winter.

This dissonance between the official calendar (January) and the natural calendar (March) is what makes many popular Carnival traditions and “Burning of Judas” still carry today a symbolism of cleansing and expulsion of the old to make way for the new, typical of ancient year-turning rituals.
