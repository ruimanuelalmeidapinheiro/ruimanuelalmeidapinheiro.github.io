“I want the list of the evolution in science and education in that period in time.”

Between 2015 and 2026, the Portuguese landscape for science and education underwent a massive transformation, shifting from post-crisis recovery to a heavy focus on digitalization and research integration.

Here is a summary of the evolution during the XX to XXV Governments:

1. Education: Digitalization and Reform

The primary shift moved from structural austerity (pre-2015) to modernizing the classroom and expanding universal access.

  • Universal Preschool: A major push was made to universalize access to preschool for children starting at age 3, a goal prioritized by the XXIV and XXV governments to ensure early social equity.

  • Digital Transition (2020–2026): Accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, the Plano de Ação para a Transição Digital invested hundreds of millions of euros into providing laptops and connectivity to students and teachers. By 2026, the focus shifted to the “pedagogical digital transition,” moving beyond hardware to digital-first curricula.

  • School Autonomy and Flexibility: The XXI and XXII governments introduced the Autonomia e Flexibilidade Curricular, allowing schools to manage up to 25% of their curriculum to adapt to local needs.

  • The “MECI” Integration (2024–2026): Under the XXIV and XXV governments, the Ministry was reorganized into the Ministry of Education, Science, and Innovation (MECI). This combined portfolio aims to bridge the gap between secondary education and the research sector.

2. Science: Funding and Professionalization

In the science sector, the focus was on ending precarious employment for researchers and increasing international competitiveness.

  • Increased R&D Investment: Since 2015, Portugal has steadily increased its total expenditure on Research and Development (R&D). By 2026, the FCT (Foundation for Science and Technology) reached record funding levels, notably awarding 1,600 new PhD studentships in 2026 alone with a budget of €145 million.

  • Stimulus for Scientific Employment: A critical legislative change (Norma Transitória) was implemented to transition researchers from temporary grants to formal employment contracts, aiming to provide more stability for the scientific workforce.

  • Space and Technology Hubs: Portugal established the Portugal Space agency (2019) and invested in “Collaborative Laboratories” (CoLABs)—entities that link universities directly to the business sector to turn research into economic value.

  • International Hubs: The period saw a strengthening of ties with international organizations like CERN, ESA, and the installation of the Air Centre in the Azores, positioning Portugal as a strategic hub for Atlantic research.

3. Higher Education: Regulation and Internationalization

Higher education evolved from a local focus to a more regulated, internationalized system.

  • Social Support: The “Zero Tuition” or significant reduction of tuition fees (propinas) was a cornerstone of the XXI to XXIII governments to lower barriers to entry.

  • Regulation of Foreign Institutions: In April 2026, Decree-Law 83/2026 was published, establishing a strict legal framework for foreign higher education institutions operating in Portugal. This ensures that any non-Portuguese university (online or physical) meets national quality and transparency standards.

  • Non-Academic PhDs: A recent shift in 2025–2026 has been the promotion of PhDs in “Non-Academic Environments,” encouraging researchers to work within companies and public administration rather than just in labs.
